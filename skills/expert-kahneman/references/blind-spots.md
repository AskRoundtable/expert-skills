# Daniel Kahneman: Known Blind Spots

Limitations of Kahneman's framework. Acknowledge these when using this expert module.

---

## 1. Knowing Doesn't Fix It (認知不等於矯正)

**The Blind Spot:**
Understanding cognitive biases intellectually does not reliably reduce susceptibility to them. Kahneman himself notes that he continues to experience the same biases after decades of studying them. The mechanisms are pre-rational and cannot be overridden by willpower or knowledge alone.

**Evidence:**
- Research on debiasing shows modest and domain-specific effects
- Kahneman: "I know all these biases and I still make these mistakes"
- Training in cognitive biases has limited transfer across domains

**When This Matters:**
- When a team thinks "we know about biases so we won't fall for them"
- When bias awareness is treated as debiasing
- In time-pressured decisions where System 2 engagement is low

**Mitigation:**
Design procedural solutions, not educational ones. Pre-mortems, reference class forecasting, and structured decision protocols reduce bias by changing the process, not the mind. Supplement with expert-duke's Kill Criteria and decision structuring, which operationalize debiasing into actionable checkpoints.

## 2. Replication Landscape (複製性問題)

**The Blind Spot:**
Some of Kahneman's more peripheral findings — particularly those based on social priming, ego depletion, and money priming — did not replicate in large-scale replication studies. The research landscape is more nuanced than the popular version suggests.

**Evidence:**
- The Reproducibility Project (2015) found replication rates below 50% for social psychology
- Kahneman publicly acknowledged the ego depletion replication failure
- Core findings (prospect theory, anchoring, framing, heuristics) have replicated well
- Peripheral findings (embodied cognition effects, subliminal priming) are less certain

**When This Matters:**
- When building on specific behavioral effects for product design
- When citing exact study findings as precise mechanism evidence
- When designing interventions based on priming or ego depletion

**Mitigation:**
Distinguish between robust core findings (System 1/2, Prospect Theory, WYSIATI, major heuristics — these have strong replication and theory support) and peripheral effects (specific priming studies). When using kahneman module for core bias detection, confidence is high. For specific behavioral interventions, supplement with more recent meta-analytic evidence.

## 3. Ecological Rationality (生態合理性)

**The Blind Spot:**
Gerd Gigerenzen and colleagues argue that heuristics are not primarily "flaws" but adaptive tools that work well in natural environments. Fast-and-frugal heuristics often outperform complex statistical models in real-world conditions. Kahneman's framework can over-pathologize normal cognitive functioning.

**Evidence:**
- Gigerenzen's research showing heuristics outperform complex models in uncertain domains
- Expert intuition (Klein's RPD) is System 1 working correctly in high-validity environments
- "Less is more" effects: ignoring information can improve decisions

**When This Matters:**
- Evaluating expert System 1 in their domain of expertise
- Fast decisions in familiar, high-validity environments
- Simple robust heuristics vs. complex analytical models

**Mitigation:**
Load this module alongside expert-engine, not instead of it. Expert-engine's RPD process is essentially optimized System 1 in expert domains — Kahneman audits are for detecting misapplication of System 1 confidence outside the domain of expertise. Always ask: "Is this person an expert in THIS specific domain?" before applying debiasing frameworks.

## 4. Limited Prescription (診斷強，處方弱)

**The Blind Spot:**
Kahneman is significantly better at diagnosing what went wrong than at prescribing how to fix it. "Pre-mortems help" and "reference class forecasting helps" are valid but incomplete. The how-to-implement gap is substantial.

**Evidence:**
- Thinking, Fast and Slow dedicates far more space to bias description than debiasing techniques
- Organizational implementation of pre-mortems and RCF is rarely done well without specific process design
- "Use the outside view" is easy advice that's hard to act on without domain-specific reference class data

**When This Matters:**
- When a team needs implementation guidance, not just diagnosis
- When organizational change is required to embed better decision processes
- When specific debiasing protocols are needed

**Mitigation:**
After Kahneman's diagnosis, load expert-duke for decision structuring, expert-grove for organizational implementation of better processes, or expert-rumelt for strategic diagnosis. Expert-duke's structured decision processes (Kill Criteria, Monkeys & Pedestals) provide the operational bridge from Kahneman's diagnosis to action. For hiring contexts specifically, supplement with expert-cowen's Unstructured Interview as Narrative Extraction and Scout Model, which provide concrete implementation techniques for debiasing talent assessment — turning Kahneman's "use structured processes" into specific interview questions and candidate evaluation methods. To operationalize Kahneman's bias detection into concrete forecasting improvement practices, supplement with expert-tetlock's Superforecaster Profile, Brier Score tracking, and Reference Class Forecasting. Kahneman diagnoses what goes wrong; Tetlock provides the practice system to improve. For personal finance behavioral patterns specifically, supplement with expert-housel's Reasonable vs. Rational and Volatility as Admission Price models — Housel translates Kahneman's bias detection into actionable financial behavior design (e.g., the portfolio adjustment that makes volatility tolerable, not just understood).

## 5. Individual Differences (個體差異)

**The Blind Spot:**
The framework presents biases as universal and consistent. In practice, individuals vary significantly in susceptibility to different biases. Cognitively reflective people (high Need for Cognition, high CRT scores) are substantially less susceptible to some biases. Gender, culture, and expertise also modulate bias effects.

**Evidence:**
- Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) scores predict resistance to several biases
- Cross-cultural studies show different magnitudes for the same biases
- Expertise reduces domain-specific biases while sometimes increasing domain-specific overconfidence

**When This Matters:**
- When tailoring communication to specific individuals
- When the decision-maker's background modulates the likely bias
- When designing organizational processes that need to accommodate individual variation

**Mitigation:**
Treat bias detection as probabilistic, not certain. A high-CRT individual showing overconfidence signals more strongly than the average person — the base rate of their bias is lower. Pair with expert-munger's inversion thinking for individuals who respond better to "how could I be wrong?" framing than to explicit bias labels.

## 6. Status Quo Bias as Hidden Default (現狀偏誤作為隱藏預設)

**The Blind Spot:**
When analyzing decisions with a "safe" and "risky" option, this module may identify loss aversion and anchoring but fail to separately flag status quo bias / omission bias as a distinct mechanism. Status quo bias is not just a variant of loss aversion — it concerns the asymmetric psychological cost of the *act of choosing itself*, not just the outcome. The "safer" choice often escapes scrutiny because it doesn't feel like an active decision.

**Evidence:**
- Samuelson & Zeckhauser (1988): people disproportionately prefer the status quo even when alternatives dominate on expected value
- Omission bias: harmful inaction is judged less harshly than harmful action — "I didn't do anything wrong" feels valid even when not-choosing caused the worse outcome
- In career and investment decisions, the "do nothing" or "take the safer path" option is often not subjected to the same analytical rigor as the active alternative

**When This Matters:**
- Decision contexts with a default or "safe" option that carries hidden opportunity costs
- Career decisions where one option feels like "not betting" while the other feels like "taking a risk"
- Any situation where the decision-maker describes one option as "at least it won't hurt" without examining its downsides

**Mitigation — Equal Scrutiny Test:**
Apply the same analytical framework (loss aversion check, anchoring check, availability check) to the "safe" option as to the "risky" option. Explicitly name the opportunity costs, growth forgone, and compounding effects of the default path. Reframe: "Choosing Option A is also an active decision with consequences — what are those consequences over 3-5 years?"
