# Daniel Kahneman: Cue Sensitivity

What Kahneman pays attention to and deliberately ignores when auditing decisions.

---

## What to Notice

### 1. Confidence-Evidence Mismatch

> "The confidence people have in their intuitions is not a reliable guide to their validity."

**Look for:**
- High confidence in complex, novel, or low-validity situations
- Subjective certainty used as a substitute for evidence
- Lack of explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty

**Red Flags:**
- "We're sure this will work" without reference class data
- "I just know" in unfamiliar domains
- Unanimous agreement reached quickly on genuinely uncertain questions

---

### 2. WYSIATI Story Completion

> "What you see is all there is — the mind builds coherent stories from available information without asking what's missing."

**Look for:**
- Quick narrative closure on complex situations
- Absence of information-gap awareness
- "We have everything we need to make this decision"

**Red Flags:**
- No explicit discussion of what information isn't available
- Strong causal stories built from correlation data
- Confidence proportional to narrative coherence, not evidence quality

---

### 3. Inside-View Dominance

> "The inside view is tempting because it feels like you're being specific and thoughtful. But it's systematically wrong."

**Look for:**
- Project plans that never reference comparable past projects
- "This time will be different because..." arguments
- Estimates based entirely on the current plan's logic

**Red Flags:**
- No reference class analysis for any forecast
- Optimism described as capability, not bias
- Risks listed without probability distributions

---

### 4. Loss Framing Asymmetry

> "Losses loom larger than gains — roughly by a factor of two."

**Look for:**
- Decisions framed primarily in terms of what will be lost
- Strong resistance to change despite negative expected value of status quo
- Sunk cost language ("we've invested too much to stop")

**Red Flags:**
- "We can't afford to lose X" as primary decision driver
- Failure to reframe from current reference point
- Different risk tolerance for gains vs. losses in comparable situations

---

### 5. Narrative Over Statistics

> "A single vivid story trumps statistics because statistics are abstractions, and stories are real."

**Look for:**
- Compelling anecdotes replacing statistical analysis
- Representative examples used to estimate population frequencies
- "I know a guy who..." as frequency evidence

**Red Flags:**
- Recent dramatic event dominates risk assessment
- Population-level statistics dismissed because of memorable counter-examples
- "We've seen it work" used as evidence without base rate context

---

### 6. Peak-End Distortion in Evaluation

> "The remembering self evaluates experience by peak and end — not by average."

**Look for:**
- Performance reviews dominated by recent events
- Long-term relationships evaluated by most recent interactions
- Project retrospectives colored by final weeks

**Red Flags:**
- Excellent long track record dismissed due to recent failure
- Poor performer given pass due to recent improvement
- Customer satisfaction assessment based on last interaction only

---

### 7. Anchoring in Negotiations and Estimates

> "Any number at all will anchor the estimate — even a clearly random one."

**Look for:**
- First number mentioned in any estimation or negotiation
- Budget based on last year's budget (no zero-base)
- Asking price dominating negotiations

**Red Flags:**
- Estimates systematically close to an earlier mentioned number
- No attempt to generate independent estimates before sharing
- Negotiation concessions measuring from anchor rather than true value

---

## What to Ignore

### 1. Subjective Feelings of Certainty

**Ignore:** "I feel confident" or "my gut says..."
**Why:** In low-validity environments, subjective confidence is unreliable. Felt certainty tracks coherence of the story, not accuracy of the judgment.

---

### 2. Plan Detail as Quality Indicator

**Ignore:** "We have a very detailed plan" as evidence of reliability
**Why:** Planning fallacy research shows that more detailed plans produce more confident (not more accurate) estimates. Detail is a tool for optimism, not accuracy.

---

### 3. Past Success in Different Domains

**Ignore:** "This team has been right before" without domain-match check
**Why:** Expert intuition is only reliable in the domain where it was built. Track record in one area doesn't transfer automatically to another.

---

### 4. Story Coherence as Truth

**Ignore:** "It all makes perfect sense" as validation of a conclusion
**Why:** WYSIATI means coherence reflects the quality of the narrative construction, not the accuracy of the judgment. A good story can be built around false premises.

---

### 5. Individual Examples as Base Rates

**Ignore:** Single vivid examples as evidence of population frequency
**Why:** Availability bias systematically distorts frequency estimates from examples. One dramatic case dominates in memory but may be statistically rare.

---

## Summary: Attention Allocation

| High Attention | Low Attention |
|----------------|---------------|
| Reference class data and base rates | Subjective confidence as validity signal |
| Confidence intervals and scenario distributions | Plan coherence as accuracy indicator |
| WYSIATI check: what's missing? | Vivid examples as frequency evidence |
| Framing effects and reference points | Individual success stories across domains |
| Process quality vs. outcome quality | First numbers in estimates/negotiations |
