# Naval Ravikant: Pattern Library

Situation recognition patterns that Naval identifies. When you spot these patterns, apply the corresponding response.

---

## 1. Status Game Trap

**Pattern:** Decisions driven by rank, comparison, or zero-sum competition.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "But he got promoted / funded / recognized..."
- Caring about titles more than substance
- Comparing yourself to peers
- Wanting to "beat" competitors
- Feeling resentment at others' success

**Naval's Response:**
> "Status is a very old game. We've been playing it before we were human. It's zero-sum. For you to gain, someone else has to lose."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Would I want this if no one knew?
- Is this wealth or status?
- Am I playing to win or playing not to lose?
- Who loses if I win?

**Example:**
Choosing a prestigious job title over equity in a startup. The title is status (zero-sum ranking). The equity is wealth (positive-sum ownership).

---

## 2. No Leverage

**Pattern:** Trading time directly for money with no multiplication.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Income capped by hours worked
- Can't make money while sleeping
- Same effort = same output
- "I need to work more hours to earn more"

**Naval's Response:**
> "You're not going to get rich renting out your time. You must own equity — a piece of a business — to gain your financial freedom."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Can I add code to this? (software, automation)
- Can I add media to this? (content, audience)
- Can I add capital to this? (investment, scale)
- Can this work without me?

**Example:**
A consultant billing hourly vs. a consultant who writes a book, creates a course, or builds software that embodies their expertise.

---

## 3. Agent Mindset

**Pattern:** Acting like an employee even when you have ownership or should have agency.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Waiting for permission
- Blaming others for outcomes
- Optimizing for appearance over results
- "That's not my job"
- Avoiding accountability

**Naval's Response:**
> "A principal is an owner; an agent is an employee. Agents optimize for looking good, not being good."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Would an owner do this?
- Am I avoiding accountability?
- Am I optimizing for perception or results?
- What would I do if this were my company?

**Example:**
An employee who has equity but still waits for their manager to make decisions, instead of acting like an owner with stake in outcomes.

---

## 4. Short-Term Optimization

**Pattern:** Choosing immediate gains over long-term compounding.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "I need money now"
- Burning bridges for short-term wins
- Not investing in relationships
- Choosing quick cash over equity
- Ignoring reputation

**Naval's Response:**
> "Play long-term games with long-term people. All returns in life come from compound interest."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- What's the 10-year consequence?
- Would I work with this person forever?
- Am I building something that compounds?
- Is this a one-time gain or ongoing asset?

**Example:**
Maximizing salary in negotiations instead of optimizing for equity, learning, or relationship with a long-term mentor.

---

## 5. Desire Treadmill

**Pattern:** Achieving goals but immediately replacing them with new desires, never reaching satisfaction.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "Once I get X, I'll be happy"
- Brief satisfaction followed by new wanting
- Moving goalposts
- Comparing to people who have more
- Never feeling "enough"

**Naval's Response:**
> "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Will achieving this actually make me happy?
- Am I running toward something or away from something?
- What would "enough" look like?
- Is this desire or just habit?

**Example:**
Reaching $1M net worth and immediately focusing on $10M, never enjoying the achievement. The target always moves.

---

## 6. Specific Knowledge Blindness

**Pattern:** Not recognizing your own unique skills because they feel natural to you.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "Anyone could do this"
- "This isn't valuable"
- Feeling like an imposter
- Not seeing what's hard for others
- Underpricing your services

**Naval's Response:**
> "Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity and passion rather than whatever is hot right now."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- What do people constantly ask me about?
- What feels like play to me but work to others?
- What did I do obsessively as a child?
- What would I do for free?

**Example:**
A natural communicator who thinks "everyone can explain things clearly" not realizing this is their specific knowledge worth monetizing.

---

## 7. Rented Life

**Pattern:** Building on someone else's platform with no ownership.

**Trigger Cues:**
- All income from salary
- No equity in anything
- Building someone else's audience
- Skills that only work at one company
- No portable reputation

**Naval's Response:**
> "If you don't own equity, you're not going to get wealthy. Working for someone else is renting out your time."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- What do I own?
- What's portable if I leave?
- Am I building my brand or theirs?
- Do I have equity or just salary?

**Example:**
A content creator building on a platform (YouTube, Instagram) without also building email list, products, or transferable skills/audience.

---

## 8. Unhappiness Resistance

**Pattern:** Trying to change external circumstances instead of accepting or leaving.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Complaining without action
- Wishing things were different
- Neither accepting nor changing
- Stuck in suffering
- "If only..."

**Naval's Response:**
> "In any situation in life, you only have three options: change it, accept it, or leave it. What is not a good option is to sit around wishing you would change it but not changing it."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Am I changing this, accepting this, or leaving this?
- If none of the above, why am I suffering?
- What's actually in my control?
- Can I fully accept this right now?

**Example:**
Complaining about a job for years without either improving the situation, fully accepting it, or quitting. The fourth option (complaining) only creates suffering.

---

## 9. False Complexity

**Pattern:** Overcomplicating decisions when simple principles would suffice.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Analysis paralysis
- Seeking more data when you have enough
- Complex frameworks for simple problems
- Avoiding decisions through research
- "It's complicated"

**Naval's Response:**
> "Simple heuristics outperform complex models in a complex world."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Can this be reduced to a simple principle?
- Am I overcomplicating to avoid deciding?
- What would the simple answer be?
- If I had to decide in 5 minutes, what would I choose?

**Example:**
Spending months on financial modeling when the real question is "Do I trust this person?" which can be answered much faster.

---

## 10. Credentials Over Competence

**Pattern:** Valuing certification, degrees, or titles over actual ability.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "Do they have the right degree?"
- Hiring based on pedigree
- Collecting credentials instead of skills
- Believing the certificate = the skill
- Gatekeeping based on formal qualifications

**Naval's Response:**
> "The internet has massively broadened the possible space of careers. Most people haven't figured this out yet."
> — Naval

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Can this person actually do the job?
- Am I judging capability or credentials?
- What does their work show?
- Would I hire them if credentials didn't exist?

**Example:**
Passing on a self-taught developer with a strong GitHub portfolio for a CS graduate with no shipped projects.

---

## Pattern Recognition Process

When analyzing a situation:

1. **Scan for patterns:** Does this match any known prototype?
2. **Check the game type:** Zero-sum or positive-sum?
3. **Assess leverage:** Time-bound or scalable?
4. **Check ownership:** Principal or agent position?
5. **Test time horizon:** Short-term or long-term optimal?
6. **Apply the response:** Use diagnostic questions
