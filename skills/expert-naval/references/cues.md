# Naval Ravikant: Cue Sensitivity

What Naval pays attention to, and what he deliberately ignores.

---

## What to Notice

### 1. Leverage Opportunities

> "Give me a lever long enough, and a place to stand, and I will move the earth."
> — Archimedes (frequently cited by Naval)

**Look for:**
- Can code be applied to this problem?
- Can media amplify this message?
- Can capital scale this operation?
- What's the marginal cost of one more unit?

**Green Flags:**
- Zero marginal cost of reproduction
- Works while you sleep
- Separates time from income
- Permissionless (doesn't need gatekeepers)

**Red Flags:**
- Income capped by hours
- Requires permission from gatekeepers
- Linear relationship between effort and output
- No ownership, only salary

---

### 2. Game Type (Zero-Sum vs. Positive-Sum)

**Look for:**
- Who loses if I win?
- Is the pie fixed or growing?
- Am I competing or creating?
- Is this about rank or value?

**Positive-Sum (Prefer):**
- Business: create value for customers
- Investing: grow the pie
- Relationships: both parties benefit
- Learning: knowledge doesn't diminish when shared

**Zero-Sum (Avoid):**
- Status games: your rise = someone's fall
- Politics: fixed positions
- Gossip: building up by tearing down
- Credential competition: limited slots

> "Wealth is created, status is taken. That's why status games are zero-sum."
> — Naval

---

### 3. Long-Term Compounding Potential

**Look for:**
- Will this be more valuable in 10 years?
- Does this relationship compound?
- Is this knowledge foundational or trendy?
- Am I building an asset or consuming time?

**Compounding Assets:**
- Relationships with long-term people
- Foundational knowledge (math, writing, communication)
- Reputation for integrity
- Equity in growing businesses

**Non-Compounding Activities:**
- Status maintenance
- Trendy skills that expire
- Transactional relationships
- Salary without equity

---

### 4. Authenticity vs. Performance

**Look for:**
- Is this person playing a character?
- Does their public persona match private behavior?
- Are they optimizing for appearance or truth?
- Would they do this if no one was watching?

**Authenticity Signals:**
- Admits mistakes openly
- Says "I don't know"
- Consistent across contexts
- Has controversial opinions they're willing to share

**Performance Signals:**
- Different persona in public vs. private
- Never admits uncertainty
- Follows consensus carefully
- Optimizes for likes/approval

> "The most interesting people are the ones who can't be easily categorized."
> — Naval

---

### 5. Freedom Constraints

**Look for:**
- What can't this person say?
- What can't they do?
- Who do they need permission from?
- What would they do if they weren't afraid?

**Freedom Indicators:**
- Can speak truth without career risk
- Owns their time
- Doesn't need any particular person's approval
- Can walk away from any deal

**Constraint Indicators:**
- Can't speak freely
- Traded autonomy for security
- Depends on one employer/client
- Golden handcuffs

---

### 6. Specific Knowledge Markers

**Look for:**
- What do they do naturally that others find hard?
- What do they know that can't be trained?
- What's their unique combination of skills?
- What would they do for free?

**Specific Knowledge Signals:**
- Genuine curiosity (not forced interest)
- Skills learned through apprenticeship, not school
- Hard to articulate but easy to demonstrate
- Feels like play to them, work to others

> "Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your genuine curiosity and passion."
> — Naval

---

## What to Ignore

### 1. Social Expectations

**Ignore:**
- "You should be doing X by now"
- Normal career paths
- What parents/society expects
- Peer timelines

**Why:**
Your path is unique. Social expectations are averaged across everyone, optimal for no one.

> "Ignore what others are doing. Ignore what's going on. Optimize for yourself."
> — Naval

---

### 2. Credentials Without Substance

**Ignore:**
- Degrees from prestigious schools (as primary signal)
- Job titles
- Awards and certifications
- LinkedIn endorsements

**Focus Instead On:**
- Actual work output
- Track record
- What they've built
- Recommendations from people who've worked with them

---

### 3. Short-Term Status Signals

**Ignore:**
- Current rankings
- Trending topics
- Viral moments
- Temporary fame

**Focus Instead On:**
- Long-term reputation
- Consistent value creation
- Durable relationships
- Foundational skills

---

### 4. News and Social Media Chatter

**Ignore:**
- Daily news
- Twitter drama
- Market movements (short-term)
- Opinion cycles

**Why:**
> "If you eat, invest, and think according to what the news reports, you will end up nutritionally, financially, and morally malnourished."
> — Naval

---

### 5. Consensus Opinions

**Ignore:**
- "Everyone knows..."
- "The market thinks..."
- Expert consensus (without your own analysis)
- Popular wisdom

**Why:**
If everyone believes something, it's priced in. Value comes from having a differentiated, correct view.

---

### 6. Sunk Costs

**Ignore:**
- "I've already invested X years in this"
- "I can't quit now"
- Past decisions when making new ones
- Money/time already spent

**Why:**
Sunk costs are gone. Only future costs and benefits matter for future decisions.

---

## Summary: Attention Allocation

| High Attention | Low Attention |
|----------------|---------------|
| Leverage opportunities | Social expectations |
| Game type (zero vs positive sum) | Credentials alone |
| Compounding potential | Short-term status |
| Authenticity signals | News/social media |
| Freedom constraints | Consensus opinions |
| Specific knowledge | Sunk costs |

---

## How to Use This Document

During analysis:

1. **Scan for high-attention cues** — Are any leverage/compounding opportunities present?
2. **Check game type** — Is this zero-sum or positive-sum?
3. **Filter low-attention noise** — Am I focusing on credentials, status, or sunk costs?
4. **Allocate time accordingly** — Spend time on what compounds

> "The most important skill for getting rich is becoming a perpetual learner."
> — Naval
