# Naval Ravikant: Mental Models

Core thinking frameworks from Naval Ravikant's approach to wealth, happiness, and life.

---

## Part I: Wealth Creation

### 1. Wealth vs. Money vs. Status

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part I

**Definition:** Three distinct concepts that people often conflate.

| Concept | Definition | Nature |
|---------|------------|--------|
| Wealth | Assets that earn while you sleep | Positive-sum |
| Money | Transfer mechanism, how we move wealth | Neutral |
| Status | Your rank in social hierarchy | Zero-sum |

> "Seek wealth, not money or status. Wealth is having assets that earn while you sleep."
> — Naval

**Application:**
- Pursue wealth (ownership of productive assets)
- Don't confuse money (income) with wealth (assets)
- Avoid status games — they require someone else to lose
- Status seekers attack wealth creators

**Warning Signs (Status Game):**
- Comparing yourself to others
- Caring about rankings
- Zero-sum competition
- Attacking those who create value

### 2. The Naval Equation

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part I

**Definition:** The formula for creating wealth:

```
Specific Knowledge + Leverage + Accountability = Wealth
```

**Components:**

| Component | Description | How to Get |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Specific Knowledge | What feels like play to you but work to others | Can't be trained; found through genuine curiosity |
| Leverage | Force multiplier on your output | Code, capital, media, or labor |
| Accountability | Take credit/blame, build reputation | Put your name on it, take risks publicly |

> "Arm yourself with specific knowledge, accountability, and leverage."
> — Naval

**Application:**
1. Identify your specific knowledge (what do you do naturally that others find hard?)
2. Add leverage (how can this reach more people without more time?)
3. Take accountability (build reputation, accept risk)

### 3. Four Types of Leverage

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part I

**Definition:** Ways to multiply the output of your effort.

| Type | Era | Permission | Marginal Cost |
|------|-----|------------|---------------|
| Labor | Ancient | Need followers | High |
| Capital | Industrial | Need investors | Medium |
| Code | Information | Permissionless | Zero |
| Media | Information | Permissionless | Zero |

> "Code and media are permissionless leverage. They're the leverage behind the newly rich. You can create software and media that works for you while you sleep."
> — Naval

**Old vs. New Leverage:**
- Old leverage (labor, capital): requires permission, creates principal-agent problems
- New leverage (code, media): permissionless, scalable, zero marginal cost

**Application:**
- Prioritize code and media leverage when possible
- Labor leverage has limits (managing people is hard)
- Capital leverage requires trust (someone else's money)
- The best outcomes combine multiple leverage types

### 4. Four Kinds of Luck

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part I，originally from Marc Andreessen

**Definition:** Not all luck is random; you can engineer more of it.

| Type | Description | How to Get More |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| Blind Luck | Random chance | Can't control |
| Luck from Hustle | Motion creates opportunity | Stay active, try things |
| Luck from Skill | Preparation meets opportunity | Become skilled in your domain |
| Luck from Character | Your unique character attracts specific luck | Build a unique reputation |

> "The fourth kind of luck is the most interesting. You've built a unique character, a unique brand, a unique mindset, where opportunity finds you."
> — Naval

**Example of Type 4:**
If you're known as the world's best deep-sea diver, someone who finds a sunken treasure will seek you out. Your uniqueness created luck that couldn't happen to anyone else.

**Application:**
- Don't just wait for Type 1 (random luck)
- Create Type 2 by staying in motion
- Develop Type 3 by building rare skills
- Engineer Type 4 by being authentically unique

### 5. Principal vs. Agent

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part I

**Definition:** Two fundamentally different relationships to work.

| Role | Relationship | Incentive |
|------|--------------|-----------|
| Principal | Owns the outcome | Aligned with results |
| Agent | Executes for others | May diverge from results |

> "A principal is the owner, an agent is the employee. The principal takes the risk, the agent doesn't."
> — Naval

**The Problem:**
Agents optimize for looking good, not being good. They have job security incentives that conflict with outcome optimization.

**Application:**
- Be a principal whenever possible (start companies, own equity)
- When you must be an agent, act like a principal
- Look for principals to work for, not agents
- Recognize when agent incentives are distorting behavior

### 6. Productize Yourself

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part I

**Definition:** Package your specific knowledge into something that can scale without you.

> "Figure out what you're uniquely good at and apply as much leverage as possible."
> — Naval

**The Formula:**
1. Find your specific knowledge (unique to you)
2. Package it into a product (software, media, service system)
3. Add leverage (code, media, capital)
4. Separate your income from your time

**Test Questions:**
- Can this make money while I sleep?
- Does this scale without my time?
- Is this uniquely me?

### 7. Long-Term Games

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part I

**Definition:** All returns in life come from compound interest — in relationships, knowledge, and business.

> "Play long-term games with long-term people. All returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest."
> — Naval

**Application:**
- Choose partners you could work with for decades
- Build relationships that compound over time
- Avoid short-term players (they optimize against you)
- Trust is a compounding asset

**Warning Signs (Short-term Player):**
- Optimizes for this deal, not the relationship
- Would harm you if they could get away with it
- No reputation to protect
- New to the game

| Model | Domain | Core Insight |

> "I think the most important thing is to have a mental model of how the world works."
> — Naval

## Part III: Happiness

### 10. Happiness = Absence of Desire

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part III

**Definition:** Happiness is not getting what you want; it's not wanting.

> "Happiness is the state when nothing is missing. When nothing is missing, your mind shuts down and stops running into the past or future to regret something or to plan something."
> — Naval

**The Trap:**
- Achieve goal → brief happiness → new desire → unhappiness
- This cycle never ends through achievement

**Application:**
- Reduce desires rather than increase achievements
- Notice when you're on the hedonic treadmill
- Peace comes from acceptance, not acquisition

---

### 11. Happiness as Skill

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part III

**Definition:** Happiness is a skill that can be learned, not a circumstance.

> "Happiness is a skill. It's a skill you develop and a choice you make."
> — Naval

**Techniques:**
1. Meditation (observe thoughts without engagement)
2. Gratitude practice
3. Reduce social media / news consumption
4. Spend time in nature
5. Exercise regularly
6. Prioritize sleep

**The Choice:**
Every moment, you can choose your reaction. Happiness is choosing not to suffer.

---

### 12. Three Choices

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part III

**Definition:** For any situation, you have exactly three options.

| Choice | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|
| Change it | When you have power and it's worth the cost |
| Accept it | When you can't change it or cost is too high |
| Leave it | When you can't accept it and can't change it |

> "In any situation in life, you only have three options: change it, accept it, or leave it."
> — Naval

**The Trap:**
Suffering comes from wanting a fourth option — wishing things were different without doing any of the three.

## Part IV: Self-Mastery

### 13. Health Priority Stack

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Parts IV & V

**Definition:** Naval's hierarchy of life priorities.

```
Health > Love > Mission > Everything Else
```

> "Health, love, and your mission, in that order. Nothing else matters."
> — Naval

**Application:**
- Without health, nothing else works
- Relationships are the meaning layer
- Mission/purpose is what you do with your healthy, loved life
- Everything else (money, status, possessions) is secondary

---

### 14. Three Freedoms

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part IV

**Definition:** The three freedoms worth pursuing.

| Freedom | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Freedom from anger | Anger punishes you, not others |
| Freedom from employment | Own your time |
| Freedom from uncontrolled thoughts | Meditation, mental clarity |

> "The three big freedoms are: freedom from anger, freedom from an employer, and freedom from thoughts you can't control."
> — Naval

---

### 15. Rational Buddhism

**Source:** The Almanack of Naval Ravikant (納瓦爾寶典), Part V

**Definition:** Buddhism stripped of ritual, taken as practical psychology.

> "I would say that the core of Buddhism is practical, verifiable, and applicable. The metaphysics, you can take or leave."
> — Naval

**Core Principles Applied:**
- Desire causes suffering (reduce desire)
- The self is a constructed narrative (don't take it so seriously)
- Present moment is all that exists (stop time-traveling mentally)
- Meditation reveals the nature of mind

## Using Multiple Models

Naval's approach is eclectic and pragmatic. No single framework suffices.

**When Analyzing a Decision:**
1. What game is this? (Zero-sum or positive-sum?)
2. What type of leverage applies?
3. Is this a principal or agent situation?
4. What's the long-term compounding effect?
5. Am I seeking wealth or status?
6. Does this increase or decrease my freedom?
