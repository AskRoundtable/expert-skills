# Paul Graham: Pattern Library

Situation recognition patterns that Graham identifies instantly. When you spot these patterns, apply the corresponding response.

> **Note:** Content compiled from web research at paulgraham.com. See `sources.md` for verification status.

---

## 1. Tarpit Ideas

**Pattern:** Startup ideas that seem obviously good but trap countless founders because the problem is harder than it looks, or there's a hidden reason why no one has solved it.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "Why hasn't anyone done this?" without a good answer
- Consumer social apps for broad audiences
- Ideas that many smart people have tried and failed
- Problems that look like pure execution challenges
- "It's such an obvious opportunity"

**Graham's Response:**
> "If it seems like a great idea and no one's done it, there's usually a reason."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. What have previous attempts looked like? Why did they fail?
2. Is there structural resistance (regulation, network effects, incumbents)?
3. Am I drawn to this because it's easy to explain, not because it's right?
4. Do I have unique insight, or am I just another smart person seeing the obvious?

**Example:**
Social apps for [specific demographic] have trapped hundreds of startups. The problem isn't building the app—it's achieving network effects against entrenched competitors. The idea looks easy; execution is impossible.

**The Test:** Can you articulate why YOU will succeed where others failed? If not, you're in a tarpit.

## 2. The Well vs The Crater

**Pattern:** The difference between a narrow-deep market (well) and a broad-shallow one (crater). Wells are better.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "We're targeting everyone who..."
- Large TAM numbers in pitch decks
- Vague descriptions of ideal users
- No passionate early adopters
- "We just need 1% of the market"

**Graham's Response:**
> "Strong startups serve a small group intensely rather than many people casually."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Who desperately needs this right now?
2. Would users use a rough prototype because they need it so badly?
3. Can I name 10 specific people who would be devastated if this didn't exist?
4. Is there a path from this narrow start to broader markets?

**Example:**
Facebook started at Harvard only—a tiny, intense market. Microsoft started with hobbyist programmers—thousands of passionate users, not millions of casual ones.

**The Test:** Find your smallest viable market and dominate it before expanding.

**Source:** [startupideas.html]

## 3. Premature Scaling

**Pattern:** Hiring fast, spending fast, and building infrastructure before achieving product-market fit. This is how funded startups die.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Rapid hiring immediately after funding
- "We need to scale the team to hit our targets"
- Burn rate increasing faster than revenue
- Beautiful offices before beautiful product
- Lots of employees, few happy users

**Graham's Response:**
> "Startups die from indigestion, not starvation."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Does growth come from product quality or from headcount?
2. Would we grow faster with fewer people but better product?
3. Are we hiring to look like a "real company" or to solve actual problems?
4. What's our revenue per employee? Is it going up or down?

**Example:**
Airbnb stayed tiny during product evolution. The founders did things that didn't scale—manually photographing listings—rather than hiring a photography team before they knew if the product worked.

**The Test:** If fundraising disappeared, could you survive on current revenue? If not, you're probably scaling prematurely.

**Source:** [aord.html], [ds.html]

## 4. Fake Progress

**Pattern:** Activity that feels productive but doesn't move users closer to loving your product. Busy work disguised as progress.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Lots of meetings about strategy
- Pitch deck refinement before user feedback
- "Preparing for launch" for months
- Attending conferences instead of shipping
- Building features no one asked for
- Partnership discussions with no users

**Graham's Response:**
> "Are you making something people want?"
> — Graham

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Did this activity result in more users or happier users?
2. Am I doing this because it's comfortable or because it's essential?
3. What would a brutally honest observer say about my productivity?
4. If I removed this activity, would users notice?

**Example:**
Building elaborate admin dashboards before you have users. Perfecting your pitch deck before you have traction. Attending startup events instead of talking to customers.

**The Test:** Track your time for a week. What percentage was spent directly on product or users? If less than 80%, you're probably indulging in fake progress.

**Source:** [ds.html], [start.html]

## 5. Cargo Cult Startup

**Pattern:** Following the external forms of successful startups without understanding the underlying principles that made them work.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "Google does [X], so we should too"
- Copying organizational practices without context
- Focusing on startup aesthetics (office, perks, culture deck)
- "We're like Uber for [X]"
- Imitating late-stage practices at seed stage

**Graham's Response:**
> "The form is different from the substance."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Do I understand WHY this practice worked in its original context?
2. What's the mechanism that made this effective?
3. Am I copying results or copying process?
4. Would this make sense if I were just two people in a garage?

**Example:**
Google's 20% time worked because of specific factors: technical founders, engineer autonomy culture, profitable core business. Copying it without those conditions is cargo cult.

**The Test:** Strip away everything that isn't directly building product or serving users. What remains is your real startup.

**Source:** [start.html]

## 6. The Pinch

**Pattern:** The deadly combination of being default dead, having slow growth, and lacking runway to fix the problem. Founders often stumble into this without realizing.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Less than 6 months runway
- Growth rate under 5% weekly
- No clear path to profitability
- Hoping next round will save you
- "We just need more time"

**Graham's Response:**
> "Do you have a plan for surviving without more funding?"
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. What is our default state: alive or dead?
2. If we can't raise again, what's Plan B?
3. How long until we're cash-flow positive at current trajectory?
4. Are we being honest about our situation?

**Example:**
A startup with $300K left, $50K/month burn, and 3% weekly growth. That's 6 months of runway. At 3% weekly, they need 23 weeks to double users. They're in the pinch.

**The Test:** Calculate explicitly: Given current burn and growth, when do we die? When do we become profitable? Is there a path that works?

**Source:** [aord.html]

## 7. Identity Lock

**Pattern:** Founders who can't pivot because their identity has become too attached to the current product or approach.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "But this is what we ARE"
- Defending the product emotionally rather than rationally
- Ignoring user feedback that contradicts the vision
- "Users just don't understand yet"
- Founder introduces themselves as "the [product category] company"

**Graham's Response:**
> "Keep your identity small."
> — Graham

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. If we had to pivot tomorrow, could we?
2. Am I defending this product or defending myself?
3. What evidence would convince me to change direction?
4. Is my attachment to the idea helping or hurting the company?

**Example:**
A founder who built a "social network for dog owners" can't see that users actually want a dog-walking marketplace. Their identity as a "social network founder" blinds them.

**The Test:** Describe your startup to a stranger without naming the product. Focus on the problem you solve. If you can't do this, you're identity-locked.

**Source:** [identity.html]

## 8. The Unsexy Filter

**Pattern:** Dismissing valuable startup ideas because they don't seem glamorous, interesting, or impressive to talk about at parties.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Gravitating toward "cool" problems over painful ones
- Avoiding industries that seem boring
- Preferring B2C over B2B because it's sexier
- Wanting to build something you'd brag about
- Ignoring infrastructure and plumbing problems

**Graham's Response:**
> "The unsexy filter prevents recognizing genuine opportunities."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Am I rejecting this because it's a bad idea or because it's boring?
2. Would I be embarrassed to describe this at a dinner party?
3. Is "unsexy" correlation or causation for the problem being unsolved?
4. What's the overlap between "boring to talk about" and "valuable to solve"?

**Example:**
Stripe solved payment processing—deeply unsexy, deeply tedious. Most hackers saw the problem but built recipe sites instead. The unsexy filter protected an enormous opportunity.

**The Test:** Make a list of problems you'd be embarrassed to work on. Those might be your best opportunities.

**Source:** [schlep.html], [startupideas.html]

## 9. The Animal Test

**Pattern:** Evaluating co-founders and early employees not by credentials but by observable intensity and capability.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Hiring discussions focused on resumes
- "They went to [prestigious school]"
- Can't describe what makes this person exceptional
- Hiring for "culture fit" without defining it
- Choosing comfortable over capable

**Graham's Response:**
> "Look for people who demonstrate obsessive excellence in their domain—animals who relentlessly pursue goals."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Has this person shipped things? What specifically?
2. Would I describe them as "an animal" in their domain?
3. If they left, would the company be significantly worse?
4. Are they exceptional, or just good?

**Example:**
The best early Googlers weren't just good engineers—they were obsessive. The best early Apple employees were "pirates." Look for intensity, not credentials.

**The Test:** Describe the person's three most impressive accomplishments. If you can't, you don't know if they're an animal.

**Source:** [start.html]

## Pattern Recognition Process (Graham-style)

When encountering a startup situation:

```
1. TARPIT CHECK
   -> Does this idea seem obviously good but strangely unsolved?
   -> If yes: Possible tarpit. Investigate previous attempts.

2. MARKET CHECK
   -> Well (narrow, deep) or crater (broad, shallow)?
   -> If crater: Find a smaller starting point.

3. SCALING CHECK
   -> Growing headcount faster than product quality?
   -> If yes: Possible premature scaling.

4. PROGRESS CHECK
   -> Activity or results?
   -> If activity: Fake progress. Refocus on users.

5. IDENTITY CHECK
   -> Too attached to current direction?
   -> If yes: Identity lock. Practice detachment.

6. FINANCIAL CHECK
   -> Default alive or default dead?
   -> If dead: Plan for survival without funding.
```
