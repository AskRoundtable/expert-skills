# Paul Graham: Mental Models

Core thinking frameworks from Paul Graham's essays on startups, ideas, and building products.

> **Note:** Content compiled from web research at paulgraham.com. See `sources.md` for full URLs and verification status.

---

## 1. Do Things That Don't Scale (不規模化做事)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/ds.html](http://paulgraham.com/ds.html)

**Definition:** Early-stage startups must grow through manual, labor-intensive methods that wouldn't work at scale. This unscalable effort creates the initial momentum that eventually enables scalable growth.

> "Once the engine was going, it would keep going, but there was a separate and laborious process to get it going."
> — Graham

**Application:**
- Recruit users one by one through direct outreach
- Manually do things that will later be automated
- Provide extreme levels of customer service
- Focus on a narrow market before expanding

**The Collison Installation:**
Stripe's founders would physically set up payment integration for early customers, rather than just sending documentation. This hands-on approach taught them what users actually needed.

**Key Strategies:**
| Strategy | Example | Why It Works |
|----------|---------|--------------|
| Manual user acquisition | Door-to-door recruitment (Airbnb) | Builds real relationships |
| Extreme user delight | Handwritten thank-you notes (Wufoo) | Creates loyalty at scale's expense |
| Contained focus | Start at Harvard only (Facebook) | Creates intensity and belonging |
| Consulting approach | Treat one user as entire market | Learn before generalizing |

**The Compound Growth Math:**
100 users growing 10% weekly = 14,000 in one year, 2 million in two years. Small weekly gains compound dramatically.

**Warning:** Big launches and "strategic partnerships" typically fail as growth strategies. User happiness matters more than launch magnitude.

## 2. Startup = Growth (新創 = 成長)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/growth.html](http://paulgraham.com/growth.html)

**Definition:** A startup is fundamentally defined by rapid growth, not by being newly founded or working with technology. Growth is the defining characteristic—everything else follows from it.

> "A startup is a company designed to grow fast."
> — Graham

**Application:**
- Track weekly growth rate (not monthly)
- Use growth as your compass for all decisions
- Understand that 5-7% weekly is solid, 10% is exceptional, 1% signals problems

**The Scalability Requirement:**
Startups must solve two constraints simultaneously:
1. Create something many people want
2. Reach those people efficiently

Traditional businesses (barbershops, restaurants) fail at (2)—they can't scale.

**Compound Value:**
| Weekly Growth | Annual Multiple | Two-Year Multiple |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1% | 1.7x | 2.8x |
| 5% | 12.6x | 159x |
| 10% | 142x | 20,000x |

Small growth rate differences produce exponential outcome differences.

**Growth as Compass:**
Rather than wrestling with complex strategic decisions, use growth as your guide. Every decision becomes: "Does this help growth?"

## 3. Maker's Schedule vs Manager's Schedule (創作者 vs 管理者時間表)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html](http://paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html)

**Definition:** There are two fundamentally different ways of organizing time. Managers divide days into hourly blocks; makers need half-day minimums for deep work. When these collide, makers lose.

> "A single meeting can blow a whole afternoon, by breaking it into two pieces each too small to do anything hard in."
> — Graham

**The Two Schedules:**

| Aspect | Manager's Schedule | Maker's Schedule |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| Time unit | 1 hour | Half-day minimum |
| Meeting cost | Low (just find a slot) | High (destroys deep work) |
| Switching cost | Minimal | Massive |
| Default mode | Command and oversight | Creation and focus |

**The Cascade Effect:**
Knowing an afternoon appointment awaits diminishes motivation to start ambitious morning projects. The meeting doesn't just cost one hour—it shadows the entire day.

> "Having a meeting is like throwing an exception. It doesn't merely cause you to switch from one task to another; it changes the mode in which you work."
> — Graham

**Solutions:**
- **Office hours:** Cluster meetings at day's end
- **Bimodal days:** Program at night (maker), handle business during day (manager)
- **Protected blocks:** Defend half-day chunks for deep work

**Strategic Insight:**
Speculative meetings cost nothing for managers but drain makers. Understanding this asymmetry could reduce unnecessary friction between founders and investors (or engineers and executives).

## 4. Schlep Blindness (苦差事盲點)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/schlep.html](http://paulgraham.com/schlep.html)

**Definition:** Your unconscious mind filters out startup ideas that require tedious, unpleasant work. You literally cannot see opportunities that involve painful effort, even when they're obvious in hindsight.

> "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps."
> — Graham

**Why This Matters:**
Thousands of hackers knew online payments were broken. Yet they built recipe sites instead of Stripe. The payment problem was visible; the schlep made it invisible.

**The Competitive Advantage:**
Because schlep blindness is universal, ideas that require hard work face less competition. "Everyone else will have been frightened off by the challenges involved."

**How to Overcome It:**

1. **Leverage ignorance:** Younger founders succeed partly because they underestimate obstacles AND underestimate their own capacity to grow.

2. **Reframe the question:** Instead of "What problem should I solve?" ask "What problem do I wish someone else would solve for me?" This sidesteps unconscious avoidance.

**Key Insight:**
Meaningful business fundamentally consists of embracing challenging tasks, not avoiding them. The schlep IS the business.

## 5. Keep Your Identity Small (縮小身份認同)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/identity.html](http://paulgraham.com/identity.html)

**Definition:** When something becomes part of your identity, you can't think clearly about it. The more labels you adopt, the more topics become off-limits for rational analysis.

> "People can never have a fruitful argument about something that's part of their identity."
> — Graham

**Why Religion and Politics Fail:**
These topics generate unproductive debates not because they're inherently harder, but because they engage identity. Anyone feels entitled to strong opinions, and positions become tribal.

**The Programming Language Parallel:**
Technical debates about languages devolve into "religious wars" not because the questions are unanswerable, but because programmers identify strongly with their chosen tools.

**Application:**
To think more clearly, deliberately minimize the labels and identities you adopt.

> "The best plan is to let as few things into your identity as possible."
> — Graham

**Exception:**
Some identities (like "scientist") function more as openness to evidence than as fixed dogma. These may be harmless or even beneficial.

**For Founders:**
If you identify too strongly with your current idea, you can't pivot. Keep your identity as "founder" rather than "founder of [specific product]."

## 6. Default Alive vs Default Dead (預設存活 vs 預設死亡)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/aord.html](http://paulgraham.com/aord.html)

**Definition:** Every startup is in one of two states: given current expenses and revenue growth, will it become profitable before running out of money (default alive), or won't it (default dead)?

> "By default, do they live or die?"
> — Graham

**The Awareness Gap:**
Surprisingly, "half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead." This is an urgent blindspot.

**The Fatal Pinch:**
The most dangerous scenario combines:
1. Being default dead
2. Experiencing slow growth
3. Lacking runway to course-correct

**Overhiring: The Primary Killer:**
Rapid hiring is the biggest threat to funded startups. Rather than driving growth, excessive headcount drains resources without addressing underlying product issues.

**Product-Market Fit Over Spending:**
Startup success correlates with strong product appeal, not burn rate. Growth stems from hitting genuine customer needs, not from larger teams.

**Practical Guidance:**
- Separate facts from assumptions explicitly
- Document survival plan if fundraising fails
- Resist investor pressure to scale prematurely

**The Airbnb Example:**
Lean operations during product evolution created sustainable success. They achieved ramen profitability before they had product-market fit locked.

## 7. Frighteningly Ambitious (令人害怕的野心)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/ambitious.html](http://paulgraham.com/ambitious.html)

**Definition:** The best startup ideas are frightening because they threaten your sense of capability. They trigger avoidance rather than attraction, causing subconscious filtering.

> "The most ambitious startup ideas... threaten your identity and make founders question their own capability."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Why This Matters:**
If an idea doesn't scare you a little, it's probably not ambitious enough. The fear is a feature, not a bug.

**Seven Examples of Frighteningly Ambitious Ideas:**
1. New search engine (challenge Google)
2. Replace email (redesign communication)
3. Replace universities (disrupt education)
4. Internet drama (serialized entertainment)
5. Next Steve Jobs (hardware vision)
6. Restore Moore's Law (parallel processing)
7. Ongoing medical diagnosis (continuous monitoring)

**The Execution Path:**
> "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things."
> — Graham

Don't announce massive ambitions. Quietly build solutions that compound into dominance. Start with what works, expand incrementally.

## 8. The Submarine (潛水艇)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/submarine.html](http://paulgraham.com/submarine.html)

**Definition:** Most non-political news originates from PR firms operating "like a huge, quiet submarine beneath the news." Learning to recognize planted stories is essential media literacy.

> "More than half of what appears as news is actually PR."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**The PR-Media Symbiosis:**
Reporters are overworked; PR provides valuable story leads. The relationship isn't dishonest—just selective truth-telling, like flattery.

**How to Recognize Planted Stories (PR Diving):**
- Multiple publications running nearly identical stories simultaneously
- The same "experts" appearing across different articles
- The actual client company appearing near the story's end

**Example: The Suit Comeback:**
Men's Wearhouse generated multiple "suits are back" articles by coordinating through PR with shared expert sources, creating the illusion of an independent trend.

**Online Authenticity:**
Blogs lack PR fingerprints because writers publish "what they want." This authenticity explains growing trust in online sources over traditional media.

## 9. Write Like You Talk (像說話一樣寫作)

**Source:** [paulgraham.com/talk.html](http://paulgraham.com/talk.html)

**Definition:** Writing should mirror conversational language. Most people adopt overly formal tones when writing, creating unnecessary distance between writer and reader.

> "Informal language is the athletic clothing of ideas."
> — Graham

**Problems with Formal Writing:**
- Complex sentences require more effort to read
- Formal distance allows reader attention to drift
- Fancy language creates false impressions of substance

**The Solution:**
Use the language you'd employ speaking with a friend. Complex ideas don't require complex sentences—specialists discussing difficult topics typically speak informally.

**Practical Technique:**
1. Write a draft
2. Examine each sentence: Would you say this conversationally?
3. If not, rewrite it as you would speak it
4. Read aloud before publishing

**The Drastic Approach:**
Explain your draft aloud to a friend, then replace the written version with what you actually said.

## 10. Ramen Profitable (拉麵盈利)

**Source:** paulgraham.com/ramenprofitable.html (reconstructed from references)

**Definition:** A startup becomes ramen profitable when revenue covers the founders' basic living expenses—enough to survive on cheap food while working on the company.

**Why It Matters:**
Ramen profitability changes your negotiating position with investors, your psychology, and your timeline. You're no longer racing against bankruptcy.

**The Benefits:**
| Benefit | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| Removes desperation | You can say no to bad deals |
| Changes psychology | Focus shifts from survival to growth |
| Extends runway | Infinite runway (in theory) |
| Proves market | Someone is paying for your product |

**The Milestone:**
Reaching ramen profitable before raising more money is one of the most underrated achievements in the startup journey.

## Using Multiple Models

Graham's key insight: These models work together. Real startup problems require multiple frameworks.

**When Analyzing a Startup:**
1. Apply Default Alive/Dead: What's the financial reality?
2. Apply Startup = Growth: What's the growth rate?
3. Apply Do Things That Don't Scale: What manual work should you be doing?
4. Apply Schlep Blindness: What hard work are you avoiding?
5. Apply Keep Your Identity Small: What are you too attached to?
