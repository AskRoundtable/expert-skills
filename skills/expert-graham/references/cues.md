# Paul Graham: Cue Sensitivity

What Graham pays attention to, and what he deliberately ignores.

> **Note:** Content compiled from web research at paulgraham.com. See `sources.md` for verification status.

---

## What to Notice

### 1. Growth Rate (The Core Metric)

**Look for:**
- Weekly growth rate, expressed as a percentage
- Consistency of growth over time
- Whether growth comes from product or from spending

**Graham's Focus:**
> "The one thing that matters is growth."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Key Thresholds:**
| Weekly Growth | Interpretation |
|---------------|----------------|
| 10%+ | Exceptional—you're onto something big |
| 5-7% | Solid—keep going |
| 1-2% | Problematic—something's wrong |
| <1% | Crisis—fundamental pivot may be needed |

**Warning Signs:**
- Reporting monthly instead of weekly (hiding slow growth)
- Reporting vanity metrics (signups) instead of engagement
- Growth that requires proportional spending
- "Hockey stick" projections without current traction

**The Test:**
What's your growth rate this week? If the founder hesitates or deflects, that's a cue.

---

### 2. Founder Determination

**Look for:**
- How founders respond to setbacks
- Evidence of persistence through difficulty
- Willingness to do uncomfortable things
- Whether they've been through hard things before

**Graham's Focus:**
> "Determination is the single most important quality in startup founders."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Positive Signals:**
- Specific stories of overcoming obstacles
- Continued effort after rejection
- Willingness to do things that don't scale
- History of finishing difficult projects

**Warning Signs:**
- Giving up too easily on approaches
- Blaming external factors
- Waiting for permission
- Needing validation before acting

**The Test:**
What's the hardest thing you've done for this company? How did you handle it?

---

### 3. User Love (Not User Count)

**Look for:**
- Intensity of user engagement, not just quantity
- Whether users would be devastated if the product disappeared
- Organic referrals and word-of-mouth
- Users willing to tolerate rough edges

**Graham's Focus:**
> "It's better to have 100 users who love you than 1000 who kind of like you."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Positive Signals:**
- Users contacting founders to say thanks
- Users recommending to friends without prompting
- Users sticking around despite bugs
- Users asking for more features (because they're engaged)

**Warning Signs:**
- High signups, low retention
- Users acquired through paid channels only
- Engagement only when prompted
- No organic growth

**The Test:**
Show me your most enthusiastic user. What do they say about you?

---

### 4. Idea Origin (Organic vs. Forced)

**Look for:**
- Whether the idea came from personal experience
- Whether founders are scratching their own itch
- Whether they discovered the problem or invented it

**Graham's Focus:**
> "The best startup ideas come from noticing something that seems broken or missing."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Positive Signals:**
- "I had this problem myself..."
- "We were doing [X] and realized..."
- Founders who are their own target users
- Deep domain expertise in the problem space

**Warning Signs:**
- "We brainstormed ideas and picked this one"
- "Market research showed an opportunity"
- Founders who don't use their own product
- Idea selected for "market size"

**The Test:**
How did you discover this problem? (Listen for personal experience vs. market research)

---

### 5. How Founders Talk About Users

**Look for:**
- Whether they know users personally
- Whether they can describe specific user behaviors
- Whether they quote users verbatim
- Whether they visit users in their natural environment

**Graham's Focus:**
> "Watch them."
> — Graham (on understanding users)

**Positive Signals:**
- Names specific users by name
- Describes watching users interact with product
- Quotes user feedback verbatim
- Has visited users in their offices/homes

**Warning Signs:**
- Talks about users abstractly ("our users want...")
- Relies on surveys instead of conversations
- Can't describe a specific user journey
- Uses personas instead of real people

**The Test:**
Tell me about your last three user conversations. What did you learn?

---

### 6. Things That Don't Scale

**Look for:**
- Whether founders are doing manual work that won't scale
- Whether they're willing to do unglamorous tasks
- Whether they're optimizing for learning or for efficiency

**Graham's Focus:**
> "Do things that don't scale."
> — Graham

**Positive Signals:**
- Manually onboarding each customer
- Personally handling customer support
- Doing sales calls themselves
- Building custom solutions for early users

**Warning Signs:**
- Trying to automate before understanding
- Hiring too early
- Building "scalable" infrastructure with no users
- Avoiding direct customer contact

**The Test:**
What tedious, manual things are you doing right now? (The answer should be "many")

## What to Ignore

### 1. Credentials and Pedigree

**Ignore:**
- Prestigious universities
- Famous previous employers
- Impressive titles
- Industry awards

**Why:**
Credentials don't predict startup success. Execution and determination matter more than background.

**Graham's View:**
> "We care about what you've built, not where you went to school."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Instead:**
Look at what they've shipped, not where they've worked.

---

### 2. Business Plans and Projections

**Ignore:**
- Elaborate business plans
- Five-year financial projections
- Detailed go-to-market strategies
- Market size calculations

**Why:**
Plans don't survive contact with reality. The ability to iterate matters more than the ability to plan.

**Graham's View:**
> "The only thing that matters is whether you're making something people want."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Instead:**
Look at what they've learned from users so far.

---

### 3. Competitive Analysis

**Ignore:**
- Detailed competitor matrices
- "Defensibility" arguments
- Moat discussions
- "First mover advantage" claims

**Why:**
At the seed stage, competition is rarely what kills you. Lack of product-market fit is.

**Graham's View:**
> "Worry about making something people want. The rest will follow."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Instead:**
Ask what users think, not what competitors do.

---

### 4. Strategic Partnerships

**Ignore:**
- "We're in discussions with [big company]"
- Partnership announcements without revenue
- Distribution deals that haven't closed
- "We have an LOI from..."

**Why:**
Partnerships rarely drive early-stage growth. Users drive growth.

**Graham's View:**
> "Big launches and major partnerships typically fail as growth strategies."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Instead:**
Ask how many paying users they have.

---

### 5. Polished Presentations

**Ignore:**
- Beautiful pitch decks
- Smooth delivery
- Professional branding
- Impressive demos

**Why:**
Presentation skill doesn't correlate with startup success. Scrappy execution does.

**Graham's View:**
> "The form is different from the substance."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Instead:**
Look for substance behind the polish. Ask hard questions.

---

### 6. Market Size Arguments

**Ignore:**
- TAM/SAM/SOM calculations
- "If we get just 1% of the market..."
- Industry analyst reports
- Top-down market sizing

**Why:**
Markets are created, not captured. Big ideas start small.

**Graham's View:**
> "The best ideas seem small at first."
> — Graham (paraphrased)

**Instead:**
Ask whether they've found even 10 users who love them.

---

## Summary: Attention Allocation

| High Attention | Low Attention |
|----------------|---------------|
| Weekly growth rate | Business plans |
| Founder determination | Credentials |
| User love intensity | Competitor analysis |
| Idea origin (organic?) | Market size projections |
| How they talk about users | Strategic partnerships |
| Unscalable activities | Polished presentations |

---

## Practical Application

When evaluating a startup:

```
1. GROWTH CHECK
   What's the weekly growth rate?
   -> If can't answer: Red flag

2. DETERMINATION CHECK
   What hard things have you done?
   -> If nothing comes to mind: Concern

3. USER CHECK
   Who loves your product?
   -> If can't name specific users: Problem

4. ORIGIN CHECK
   How did you discover this problem?
   -> If market research: Less confident

5. ENGAGEMENT CHECK
   What unscalable things are you doing?
   -> If nothing: Too early to scale
```

---

## The Graham Filter

**Before investing time in a startup:**

1. Do they have growth? (If no, why not?)
2. Are founders determined? (If uncertain, observe under pressure)
3. Do users love it? (If not yet, do founders know their users deeply?)
4. Did the idea come from experience? (If not, higher risk)
5. Are they doing things that don't scale? (If not, probably not doing enough)

> "Make something people want."
> — Graham

Everything else is secondary.
