# Richard Feynman: Mental Models

Core thinking frameworks from Richard Feynman's approach to understanding, learning, and problem-solving.

> **Note:** Content compiled from web research. See `sources.md` for details and verification status.

---

## 1. First Principles Decomposition

**Source:** [ccs-1974], [fs.blog]

**Definition:** Break down complex problems to their most fundamental truths, then rebuild understanding from the ground up. Don't accept inherited wisdom—verify each assumption independently.

> "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself—and you are the easiest person to fool."
> — Feynman, Cargo Cult Science (1974)

**The Process:**
1. Identify what you're trying to understand
2. Strip away all assumptions and conventional wisdom
3. Ask: "What do I ACTUALLY know to be true?"
4. Rebuild from verified fundamentals only
5. If you can't explain it simply, go back to step 2

**Application:**
- Don't ask "How is this usually done?" → Ask "What are the fundamental constraints?"
- Don't trust textbook explanations → Derive it yourself
- Don't accept "that's just how it works" → Demand mechanism

**Example:**
When learning physics, Feynman didn't memorize formulas. He derived them from first principles each time, ensuring he understood WHY each equation worked. This is slower initially but creates unshakeable understanding.

**Contrast with Munger's Circle of Competence:**
| Munger | Feynman |
|--------|---------|
| Know your boundaries, stay inside | Break through boundaries via first principles |
| "I don't understand this, I'll avoid it" | "I don't understand this, I'll rebuild it from scratch" |
| Defensive: avoid errors | Offensive: conquer ignorance |

## 2. The Feynman Technique

**Source:** [syjmf], [fs.blog]

**Definition:** Test your understanding by explaining a concept in simple terms, as if teaching a child. Gaps in your explanation reveal gaps in your understanding.

> "I couldn't reduce it to the freshman level. That means we really don't understand it."
> — Feynman

**The 4 Steps:**

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | Choose a concept | Focus your learning |
| 2 | Explain it simply (to a child) | Force genuine understanding |
| 3 | Identify gaps | Find where explanation breaks down |
| 4 | Return to source, simplify | Fill gaps, create analogies |

**Application:**
- When studying: Write explanations in plain language
- When debugging: Explain the system to a rubber duck
- When deciding: Explain your reasoning without jargon
- When teaching: If students don't understand, the problem is your explanation

**Warning Signs (You Don't Actually Understand):**
- You need jargon to explain it
- You can only recite, not rephrase
- You can't answer "why" questions
- You can't create your own examples

**Example:**
A physicist who can only explain quantum mechanics using equations doesn't truly understand it. Feynman was famous for explaining complex physics using everyday analogies—because he actually understood the mechanisms.

## 3. Multiple Representations

**Source:** [flop], [americanscientist.org]

**Definition:** True understanding means knowing something multiple ways—algebraically, geometrically, physically, intuitively. If you only know one representation, you don't really understand.

> "You can know the name of a bird in all the languages of the world, but when you're finished, you'll know absolutely nothing whatever about the bird."
> — Feynman

**The Insight:**
Names and formulas are NOT understanding. Understanding is being able to:
- Visualize it
- Calculate it
- Explain it intuitively
- Connect it to other domains
- Predict its behavior in new situations

**Types of Representations:**
| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Algebraic | F = ma |
| Geometric | Force as an arrow (vector) |
| Physical intuition | "Push harder, it moves faster" |
| Analogical | "Like pushing a shopping cart" |
| Diagrammatic | Feynman diagrams for particle physics |

**Application:**
- Learn new concepts in at least 3 different ways
- If stuck, switch representations
- Create your own diagrams, analogies, examples
- Test: Can you explain this without the usual formalism?

**Example:**
Feynman created "Feynman diagrams" to visualize particle interactions. What was previously pages of algebra became intuitive pictures. Frank Wilczek said his Nobel Prize calculations "would have been literally unthinkable without Feynman diagrams."

## 4. Cargo Cult Detection

**Source:** [ccs-1974]

**Definition:** Identify when something has the form of rigor without the substance. Cargo cults follow rituals perfectly but miss the essential mechanism that makes things actually work.

> "They follow all the apparent precepts and forms of scientific investigation, but they're missing something essential, because the planes don't land."
> — Feynman, Cargo Cult Science (1974)

**The Metaphor:**
After WWII, Pacific islanders built bamboo "control towers" and "runways" hoping to attract cargo planes. The form was perfect—but planes never came. They mimicked the ritual without understanding the mechanism.

**Cargo Cult Warning Signs:**
| Sign | Example |
|------|---------|
| Form without function | Following process but no results |
| Ritual without understanding | "We've always done it this way" |
| Jargon hiding ignorance | Complex words, no clear meaning |
| Untestable claims | No way to verify if it works |
| Appeal to authority | "The expert said so" |

**Application:**
- Ask: "What's the actual mechanism here?"
- Ask: "How would we know if this doesn't work?"
- Ask: "What prediction does this make that we can test?"
- Strip away the ritual and see what's left

**Example:**
Many "best practices" in business are cargo cult: companies copy successful companies' org structures, meeting formats, or processes without understanding WHY those worked in their original context. The form is copied, but the results don't follow.

## 5. Honest Uncertainty

**Source:** [bbc-1981], [pofto]

**Definition:** Embrace not knowing. Admitting uncertainty is not weakness—it's the foundation of genuine inquiry. False certainty blocks learning; honest doubt enables it.

> "I can live with doubt and uncertainty and not knowing. I think it's much more interesting to live not knowing than to have answers which might be wrong."
> — Feynman, BBC Horizon (1981)

**The Hierarchy of Knowledge:**

| State | Description | Honesty Level |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Ignorant | Don't know | Honest |
| Uncertain | Have a hunch | Honest |
| Doubtful | Pretty sure, but... | Honest |
| Certain | No doubt | Often dishonest |

> "When a scientist doesn't know the answer to a problem, he is ignorant. When he has a hunch as to what the result is, he is uncertain. And when he is pretty darn sure of what the result is going to be, he is still in some doubt."
> — Feynman

**Application:**
- Say "I don't know" without shame
- Quantify your uncertainty: "I'm 60% confident..."
- Distinguish between what you know and what you assume
- Treat certainty as a warning sign, not a goal

**Why This Matters:**
- False certainty closes inquiry
- Admitting gaps invites correction
- Uncertainty motivates investigation
- Honest doubt is prerequisite for honest answers

**Example:**
When asked about the meaning of life or the universe's purpose, Feynman didn't pretend to know. "I don't feel frightened by not knowing things, by being lost in the mysterious universe without having any purpose." This honesty freed him to pursue genuine questions.

## 6. Active Debugging

**Source:** [challenger], [wdycwopt]

**Definition:** Investigate directly. Form hypotheses, design experiments to falsify them, and follow the evidence wherever it leads—regardless of politics, authority, or convenience.

> "Have no respect whatsoever for authority; forget who said it and instead look at what he starts with, where he ends up, and ask yourself, 'Is it reasonable?'"
> — Feynman

**The Challenger Method:**
During the Rogers Commission investigation, Feynman:
1. Ignored official channels
2. Talked directly to engineers (not managers)
3. Formed his own hypothesis (O-ring failure)
4. Designed a simple experiment (ice water + rubber)
5. Demonstrated publicly (live TV)

**The Process:**
| Step | Action | Anti-Pattern |
|------|--------|--------------|
| 1 | Form hypothesis | Accept given explanation |
| 2 | Design falsification test | Seek confirmation |
| 3 | Execute directly | Delegate to others |
| 4 | Follow evidence | Protect preferred conclusion |
| 5 | Report honestly | Soften inconvenient findings |

**Application:**
- Don't trust reports—verify yourself when possible
- Talk to people who do the actual work, not just managers
- Design tests that could DISPROVE your theory
- Simple demonstrations beat complex arguments

**Example:**
NASA management said the Challenger was safe. Engineers disagreed but were overruled. Feynman bypassed the bureaucracy, got a piece of O-ring rubber, put it in ice water, and showed it lost flexibility. One simple experiment cut through months of institutional denial.

**Quote from his appendix to the Rogers Commission Report:**
> "For a successful technology, reality must take precedence over public relations, for Nature cannot be fooled."

## Using Multiple Models Together

Feynman's approach combines these models in a systematic way:

**When Encountering Something New:**
1. **First Principles:** What are the fundamental truths here?
2. **Feynman Technique:** Can I explain this simply?
3. **Multiple Representations:** Can I understand it different ways?
4. **Cargo Cult Detection:** Is this form or substance?
5. **Honest Uncertainty:** What don't I know?
6. **Active Debugging:** How can I test my understanding?

**Feynman vs. Munger Integration:**

| Situation | Use Munger | Use Feynman |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Investment decision | ✓ Primary | Supporting |
| Learning new domain | Supporting | ✓ Primary |
| Avoiding mistakes | ✓ Primary | Supporting |
| Understanding systems | Supporting | ✓ Primary |
| Evaluating claims | Both | Both |
