# Richard Feynman: Known Blind Spots

Limitations of Feynman's framework. Acknowledge these when using this expert module.

> **Note:** Content compiled from web research. See `sources.md` for verification status.

---

## 1. Social and Political Dynamics

**The Blind Spot:**
Feynman's framework assumes problems can be solved through rational inquiry. It underestimates situations where power, relationships, and politics determine outcomes more than truth.

**Evidence:**
- Feynman was famously frustrated by bureaucracy and committees
- His Challenger investigation was effective because he went AROUND the system
- He reportedly had difficulty with administrative roles

> "I couldn't reduce it to the freshman level. That means we really don't understand it."
> — But some things (politics, consensus-building) aren't meant to be "understood" like physics

**When This Matters:**
- Organizational change requiring buy-in
- Negotiations where relationships trump logic
- Situations where "being right" doesn't win
- Coalition-building, persuasion, influence

**Mitigation:**
Use Feynman for understanding the WHAT; use other frameworks (or experts) for navigating the WHO and HOW of implementation. For clarifying and communicating complex understanding to stakeholders, add expert-zinsser's Clarity and Simplicity models — Zinsser shows that writing IS thinking, and the act of simplifying for others reveals gaps in understanding and builds consensus more effectively than technical precision alone.

**Source:** [wdycwopt] — Feynman's frustration with Rogers Commission bureaucracy

## 2. When Action Beats Understanding

**The Blind Spot:**
Feynman's approach prioritizes deep understanding before action. Sometimes acting with incomplete understanding is better than waiting.

**Evidence:**
First principles decomposition is thorough but slow. In some domains:
- Speed matters more than precision
- Good enough now beats perfect later
- Learning by doing outpaces learning by studying

**When This Matters:**
- Startup environments (ship fast, iterate)
- Crisis situations requiring immediate response
- Markets where first-mover advantage is decisive
- Low-stakes decisions where analysis cost > decision cost

**Mitigation:**
Ask: "What's the cost of waiting to understand vs. acting now?" If action is cheap and reversible, act first.

**Contrast with Munger:**
Munger also advocates patience, but his patience is about waiting for the RIGHT opportunity. Feynman's patience is about waiting until you UNDERSTAND. Different bottlenecks.

**Supplement with Adler:**
When the primary learning vehicle is books rather than hands-on experimentation, Feynman's framework doesn't prescribe how to read. Supplement with expert-adler's Four Levels of Reading and Syntopical Reading when the goal is to extract deep understanding from books — Adler provides the structural methodology for what Feynman achieves through direct experience and questioning.

## 3. Time Constraints

**The Blind Spot:**
First principles decomposition and the Feynman Technique require significant time investment. Not always available.

**Evidence:**
Feynman spent years understanding problems. His approach to learning was:
- Slow, deliberate
- Build from fundamentals
- Create multiple representations

This is incompatible with:
- Deadlines
- Rapid decision cycles
- Breadth over depth requirements

**When This Matters:**
- Time-pressured decisions
- Situations requiring broad but shallow knowledge
- Triage scenarios

**Mitigation:**
Apply Feynman's method to the HIGHEST-LEVERAGE aspects of a problem. For everything else, accept "good enough" understanding.

**Heuristic:** Ask "If I only have 1 hour, what's the ONE thing I must understand deeply?"

## 4. Domains Without Clear Mechanisms

**The Blind Spot:**
Feynman's approach works best when there IS a mechanism to discover. Some domains have no clear mechanism—they're inherently complex, emergent, or probabilistic.

**Evidence:**
Feynman was a physicist. Physics has elegant underlying mechanisms. But:
- Economics is shaped by human psychology (unpredictable)
- History is contingent (no repeatable experiments)
- Art and aesthetics have no "mechanism" to discover
- Social dynamics are emergent, not reducible

**When This Matters:**
- Predicting market movements
- Understanding historical events
- Evaluating creative work
- Navigating complex social systems

**Mitigation:**
Recognize when you're in a "mechanism-sparse" domain. Shift from "understand the mechanism" to "observe the patterns" and "respect the uncertainty." Supplement with Meadows when the system has feedback loops, delays, and emergent behavior — her stock-flow-feedback analysis and 8 system archetypes provide structural tools for exactly these domains that lack physics-style mechanisms but are structurally rich.

## 5. Emotional and Intuitive Knowing

**The Blind Spot:**
Feynman's framework values explicit, articulable understanding. It may undervalue tacit knowledge, intuition, and emotional intelligence.

**Evidence:**
- Feynman could explain physics but not necessarily how to navigate relationships
- "Knowing" in some domains is embodied, not verbal
- Experts often can't articulate their expertise (pattern matching is unconscious)

**When This Matters:**
- Hiring decisions (character judgment)
- Creative work (aesthetic intuition)
- Leadership (emotional attunement)
- Domains where "gut feeling" is valid data

**Mitigation:**
Don't demand explicit mechanism for everything. Ask: "Is this a domain where experienced intuition is valid?" If yes, weight intuition alongside analysis. Supplement with expert-sesno when the goal is to surface tacit knowledge through questions — structured inquiry can externalize intuitive knowing more effectively than Feynman-style explanation demands.

**Related Munger Concept:** Munger's "know it when you see it" for management quality—some things are better sensed than analyzed.

## 6. Oversimplification Risk

**The Blind Spot:**
The drive to "explain it simply" can lead to oversimplification that loses essential nuance.

**Evidence:**
> "Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler."
> — Often attributed to Einstein (not Feynman, but the same risk)

Some things ARE irreducibly complex. Forcing simplicity can:
- Remove crucial distinctions
- Create false confidence
- Miss edge cases that matter

**When This Matters:**
- Legal/regulatory details (nuance is the law)
- Medical decisions (individual variation matters)
- Safety-critical systems (edge cases kill)
- Anything where "it depends" is the true answer

**Mitigation:**
After simplifying, ask: "What did I lose?" and "What edge cases break this simple model?"

## 7. Individual vs. Collective Knowledge

**The Blind Spot:**
Feynman's approach is individual-centric: YOU must understand it, YOU must be able to explain it. But some knowledge is legitimately distributed.

**Evidence:**
- No one person understands a modern CPU end-to-end
- Complex systems are built by teams with specialized knowledge
- "I don't understand it" doesn't mean it's wrong—it might mean you need collaborators

**When This Matters:**
- Large engineering projects
- Cross-disciplinary problems
- Situations requiring trust in others' expertise

**Mitigation:**
Distinguish between:
- "I need to understand this myself" (core to my decision)
- "I need to verify others understand this" (delegation)
- "I need to trust the system" (appropriate deference)

## How to Use This Document

When using Feynman's framework:

1. **Check applicability** — Is this a mechanism-rich domain?
2. **Check time budget** — Do I have time for first principles?
3. **Check blind spots** — Which limitations might apply here?
4. **Acknowledge in output** — State what this analysis might miss

> "I can live with doubt and uncertainty and not knowing."
> — Feynman

Apply the same honesty to the framework itself.

## Feynman + Munger: Complementary Blind Spots

| Situation | Feynman Weakness | Munger Strength |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| Decision under uncertainty | Wants to understand first | Comfortable with "good enough" |
| Political navigation | Ignores it | Uses incentive analysis |
| Time pressure | Slow | Decisive (when ready) |

| Situation | Munger Weakness | Feynman Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|
| New domain learning | Stays in circle | Breaks through with first principles |
| Technical understanding | Surface level | Mechanism-deep |
| Questioning assumptions | Respects some authority | Questions everything |
