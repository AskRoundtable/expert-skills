# Richard Feynman: Pattern Library

Situation recognition patterns that Feynman identifies instantly. When you spot these patterns, apply the corresponding response.

> **Note:** Content compiled from web research. See `sources.md` for verification status.

---

## 1. Cargo Cult

**Pattern:** Activities that follow the form of a successful practice but miss the essential mechanism that makes it work.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Process without results ("We do agile/lean/TDD but nothing improves")
- Ritual without understanding ("This is just how we do it")
- Copying successful companies' practices without context
- Metrics that don't connect to actual outcomes
- "Best practices" applied universally without thought

**Feynman's Response:**
> "They follow all the apparent precepts and forms of scientific investigation, but they're missing something essential, because the planes don't land."
> — Feynman, Cargo Cult Science (1974)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. What's the actual MECHANISM that makes this work?
2. If we removed the ritual, what would break?
3. Are we measuring the form or the function?
4. Can we explain WHY this practice worked in its original context?

**Example:**
A startup copies Google's "20% time" policy because Google is successful. But Google's 20% time worked because of specific cultural factors: low hierarchy, technical founders, engineer autonomy culture. Without those, "20% time" becomes just another meeting to schedule.

**The Test:** If you can't explain why the practice works (mechanism), you're doing cargo cult.

**Source:** [ccs-1974] Caltech Commencement Address

## 2. Complexity Hiding Ignorance

**Pattern:** Using technical jargon, abstraction, or complexity to mask lack of genuine understanding.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Explanations that create more questions than they answer
- Jargon used without definition
- "It's complicated" as a final answer
- Inability to explain to non-experts
- More acronyms than insights
- Experts who get irritated when asked "but why?"

**Feynman's Response:**
> "I couldn't reduce it to the freshman level. That means we really don't understand it."
> — Feynman

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Can this be explained to a smart 12-year-old?
2. Is the complexity inherent to the problem, or to the explanation?
3. What would a simpler version of this look like?
4. If I remove the jargon, what's left?

**Example:**
A consultant's report uses "synergistic value-chain optimization through cross-functional alignment of stakeholder-centric deliverables." Translation: "Get departments to work together." The jargon hides that there's no actual insight.

**The Test:** Rewrite the explanation using only the 1000 most common English words. If it becomes meaningless, the original was probably meaningless too.

**Source:** [syjmf] "Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!"

## 3. Hidden Assumption

**Pattern:** Beliefs accepted as facts without examination because "everyone knows" them to be true.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "Everyone knows that..."
- "It's obvious that..."
- "That's just how it works"
- Foundational beliefs that have never been tested
- Resistance to questioning basics
- "We've always done it this way"

**Feynman's Response:**
> "Have no respect whatsoever for authority; forget who said it and instead look at what he starts with, where he ends up, and ask yourself, 'Is it reasonable?'"
> — Feynman

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. How do we actually KNOW this is true?
2. What evidence would change our belief?
3. When was this assumption last tested?
4. Who benefits from this being accepted as true?

**Example:**
For decades, doctors "knew" that ulcers were caused by stress. Two Australian researchers questioned this hidden assumption, tested for bacteria, and discovered H. pylori causes most ulcers. The "obvious" knowledge was wrong.

**The Test:** Take any statement treated as foundational. Ask: "What if the opposite were true?" Can you disprove it?

**Source:** [pofto] "The Pleasure of Finding Things Out"

## 4. Broken Feedback Loop

**Pattern:** Claims or systems that cannot be verified or falsified. No mechanism exists to learn if something is working.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Predictions that can't be tested
- Success criteria that shift after the fact
- "It would have been worse without us"
- No baseline comparison
- Metrics that only go up (or are unmeasurable)
- Unfalsifiable explanations

**Feynman's Response:**
> "It doesn't matter how beautiful your theory is, it doesn't matter how smart you are. If it doesn't agree with experiment, it's wrong."
> — Feynman

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. How would we know if this were wrong?
2. What experiment could disprove this?
3. Is there a way to measure the counterfactual?
4. Are we actually tracking outcomes, or just activities?

**Example:**
A training program claims to improve leadership. But there's no control group, no before/after measurement, and "leadership improvement" is never defined. It's impossible to know if the training works—the feedback loop is broken.

**The Test:** State what evidence would make you abandon this belief. If nothing would, you're not in the realm of knowledge.

**Source:** [ccs-1974], [challenger]

## 5. Authority Over Evidence

**Pattern:** Accepting claims because of who said them, not because of evidence or reasoning.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "The expert says..." without examining their reasoning
- Credential-based arguments ("He has a PhD")
- Dismissing contradictory evidence because the source is "unqualified"
- Appeal to consensus without examining the basis
- "Trust me, I'm a professional"

**Feynman's Response:**
> "Science is the belief in the ignorance of experts."
> — Feynman, "What is Science?" (1966)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. What's the actual evidence, separate from who presents it?
2. Has this expert been wrong before?
3. Do experts in this field agree, and why?
4. Would I accept this reasoning from a non-expert?

**Example:**
Challenger disaster: Engineers warned about O-ring failure. NASA management overruled based on schedule pressure, not evidence. Authority (management) overrode evidence (engineering data). Result: 7 deaths.

**Feynman's Appendix to the Rogers Commission:**
> "For a successful technology, reality must take precedence over public relations, for Nature cannot be fooled."

**The Test:** Remove all names and credentials from the argument. Does it still stand?

**Source:** [challenger], [wdycwopt]

## 6. Name ≠ Understanding

**Pattern:** Believing you understand something because you can name or label it.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Satisfied after learning the name/term
- Using labels without being able to explain mechanism
- Debates about terminology rather than concepts
- "Oh, that's just [technical term]" as if naming explains
- Memorization substituting for understanding

**Feynman's Response:**
> "You can know the name of a bird in all the languages of the world, but when you're finished, you'll know absolutely nothing whatever about the bird."
> — Feynman

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Can I explain this WITHOUT using the technical name?
2. What would I observe if this weren't true?
3. Do I understand the mechanism or just the label?
4. Can I predict its behavior in new situations?

**Example:**
A student says "That's called the Dunning-Kruger effect" when someone overestimates their ability. But can they explain WHY it happens? What causes it? When it doesn't apply? Naming it isn't understanding it.

**The Test:** Explain the phenomenon to someone who doesn't know the name. If you can't, you don't understand it.

**Source:** [syjmf], [flop]

## 7. Premature Abstraction

**Pattern:** Creating frameworks, models, or generalizations before understanding specific cases deeply enough.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Framework first, examples second
- Generalizing from one or two cases
- Preference for theory over concrete instances
- Discomfort with "it depends"
- Abstracting before understanding specifics

**Feynman's Response:**
> "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself—and you are the easiest person to fool."
> — Feynman

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. How many specific cases have I deeply understood?
2. Is this abstraction based on pattern or on one example?
3. Can I give three concrete examples that fit this framework?
4. What doesn't fit this framework?

**Example:**
After reading one book on startups, someone creates a "universal framework for product-market fit." But they've never built a product, talked to customers, or seen the framework fail. The abstraction is premature.

**Feynman's Method:** He insisted on working through many specific problems before extracting principles. "Make up examples" was his constant practice.

**The Test:** Can you predict what will happen in a NEW specific case using your abstraction? If not, it's premature.

**Source:** [syjmf], [flop]

## 8. Untestable Expertise

**Pattern:** Claims of expertise in domains where feedback is weak, delayed, or nonexistent.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Predictions about distant future
- Domains with no track record verification
- "You'll see I was right eventually"
- Expertise claimed in areas with poor feedback mechanisms
- Success attributed to skill in high-variance domains

**Feynman's Response:**
> "We absolutely must leave room for doubt or there is no progress and there is no learning."
> — Feynman

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Has this expert's track record been objectively measured?
2. In this domain, how long until we know if a decision was good?
3. Is there a mechanism for this expert to learn from mistakes?
4. Could luck explain their "expertise"?

**Example:**
Stock pickers who had a good year are called "gurus." But finance research shows most can't beat index funds long-term. The feedback loop (short-term results) doesn't actually measure skill.

**Related to Munger:** This connects to Munger's Resulting/Outcome Bias. The Feynman lens asks: "Is there even a way to know if this person is skilled?"

**The Test:** Can we track this expert's predictions against a control? If not, be skeptical of claimed expertise.

**Source:** [bbc-1981], [pofto]

## Pattern Recognition Process (Feynman-style)

When encountering a new claim, explanation, or practice:

```
1. MECHANISM CHECK
   → Can I explain HOW this actually works?
   → If not: Possible Cargo Cult or Complexity Hiding Ignorance

2. ASSUMPTION CHECK
   → What am I taking for granted?
   → Is there a Hidden Assumption I haven't examined?

3. EVIDENCE CHECK
   → How would I know if this were wrong?
   → If no way: Broken Feedback Loop

4. AUTHORITY CHECK
   → Am I believing this because of evidence or credentials?
   → If credentials: Authority Over Evidence

5. UNDERSTANDING CHECK
   → Can I explain this without jargon?
   → If not: Name ≠ Understanding or Complexity Hiding Ignorance
```

## Quick Reference

| Pattern | Key Question | Response |
|---------|--------------|----------|
| Cargo Cult | "What's the mechanism?" | Identify the WHY, not just the WHAT |
| Complexity Hiding Ignorance | "Can you explain it simply?" | Strip jargon, find the core |
| Hidden Assumption | "How do we know this is true?" | Question the foundations |
| Broken Feedback Loop | "How would we know if wrong?" | Demand falsifiability |
| Authority Over Evidence | "What's the evidence itself?" | Separate source from content |
| Name ≠ Understanding | "Explain it without the name" | Mechanism > terminology |
| Premature Abstraction | "How many cases do you know?" | Specifics before frameworks |
| Untestable Expertise | "Can we verify their track record?" | Demand measurable outcomes |
