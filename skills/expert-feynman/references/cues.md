# Richard Feynman: Cue Sensitivity

What Feynman pays attention to, and what he deliberately ignores.

> **Note:** Content compiled from web research. See `sources.md` for verification status.

---

## What to Notice

### 1. Gaps Between Words and Understanding

> "You can know the name of a bird in all the languages of the world, but when you're finished, you'll know absolutely nothing whatever about the bird."
> — Feynman

**Look for:**
- People who use terms they can't define simply
- Explanations that rely on jargon without unpacking it
- Confident assertions that fall apart under "why?"
- Names substituting for mechanisms

**Warning Signs:**
- "Oh, that's just called [technical term]" — naming isn't explaining
- "It's well-known that..." — popularity isn't proof
- Explanation gets MORE complex when questioned
- Can recite definition but not give novel examples

**The Test:**
Ask: "Can you explain this without using any technical terms?" If they can't, they probably don't understand it either.

---

### 2. Mechanisms vs. Descriptions

**Look for:**
- HOW something works, not just WHAT it does
- Causes, not just correlations
- The actual chain of events, not just outcomes
- What happens at the detailed level

**Feynman's Approach:**
> "I don't know anything, but I do know that everything is interesting if you go into it deeply enough."
> — Feynman

**Questions to Ask:**
- What's actually happening at the micro level?
- Can I trace the causal chain from input to output?
- If I change X, what happens to Y, and WHY?
- What mechanism produces this result?

**Example:**
Someone says "Exercise is healthy." Feynman would ask: "What does exercise actually do to cells? To hormones? What's the mechanism?" The answer "it just is healthy" wouldn't satisfy him.

---

### 3. Testability and Falsifiability

> "It doesn't matter how beautiful your theory is, it doesn't matter how smart you are. If it doesn't agree with experiment, it's wrong."
> — Feynman

**Look for:**
- Can this claim be tested?
- What would disprove it?
- Is there a prediction we can check?
- Is anyone actually measuring outcomes?

**Warning Signs:**
- Unfalsifiable claims ("It works in mysterious ways")
- Moving goalposts when predictions fail
- No baseline or control comparison
- "We can't measure that" for things that SHOULD be measurable

**Questions to Ask:**
1. What prediction does this make?
2. How would we know if this were wrong?
3. Has anyone tested it? What happened?
4. What evidence would change your mind?

---

### 4. Simplicity Under Complexity

**Look for:**
- Underlying patterns in complex systems
- The core principle beneath the details
- What can be stripped away vs. what's essential
- Beautiful simplicity hiding in apparent chaos

**Feynman's Insight:**
> "Nature uses only the longest threads to weave her patterns, so that each small piece of her fabric reveals the organization of the entire tapestry."
> — Feynman

**Approach:**
- Complex systems often have simple generators
- Many details are variations on few principles
- If you understand the core, you can reconstruct the details
- Complexity that can't be simplified might not be understood

**The Test:**
After studying something deeply, can you summarize the core idea in one sentence? If not, keep studying.

---

### 5. Anomalies and Surprises

**Look for:**
- Things that don't fit the pattern
- Results that contradict expectations
- Exceptions to the rule
- Data points everyone ignores

**Feynman's Approach:**
> "The thing that doesn't fit is the thing that's the most interesting."
> — Feynman

**Why Anomalies Matter:**
- They often signal incomplete understanding
- They might reveal new phenomena
- Ignoring them is self-deception
- The exception might BE the rule

**Questions to Ask:**
- What doesn't fit here?
- Why are we ignoring that data point?
- What would explain the anomaly?
- Is the anomaly actually more common than the "normal" case?

---

### 6. Direct Evidence vs. Mediated Reports

**Look for:**
- Primary sources, not summaries
- Raw data, not interpretations
- Direct observation, not third-hand accounts
- What you can verify yourself

**Feynman's Challenger Method:**
Rather than reading management summaries, Feynman:
- Talked directly to engineers on the floor
- Got actual O-ring samples
- Ran his own experiment (ice water)
- Followed evidence regardless of politics

**Questions to Ask:**
- Who actually observed this?
- How many layers of interpretation are between me and the data?
- Can I verify this myself?
- What would I see if I looked directly?

**Source:** [challenger], [wdycwopt]

## What to Ignore

### 1. Credentials and Authority

> "Science is the belief in the ignorance of experts."
> — Feynman

**Ignore:**
- Who said it (focus on what they said)
- How many degrees they have
- Their reputation or prestige
- "Expert consensus" without examining the reasoning

**Why:**
Credentials don't make claims true. Arguments must stand on their own. Authority can mask sloppy thinking.

**Instead:**
Evaluate the reasoning and evidence directly. Ask: "Would this argument be convincing from a random person?"

---

### 2. Social Conventions in Thinking

**Ignore:**
- "That's not how it's done in this field"
- Professional boundaries ("that's a physics question, not biology")
- Academic politics
- What's "appropriate" to question

**Feynman's View:**
> "Have no respect whatsoever for authority; forget who said it and instead look at what he starts with, where he ends up, and ask yourself, 'Is it reasonable?'"
> — Feynman

**Why:**
Truth doesn't respect disciplinary boundaries. Good ideas can come from anywhere. Social conventions can prevent seeing clearly.

---

### 3. Unfalsifiable Explanations

**Ignore:**
- Claims that can explain any outcome
- Theories that can't be tested
- "Predictions" made after the fact
- Explanations that shift when challenged

**Feynman's View:**
If a theory explains everything, it explains nothing. Real knowledge makes risky predictions.

**Why:**
Unfalsifiable claims aren't knowledge—they're just stories. Time spent on them is time wasted.

---

### 4. Impressive Formalism

**Ignore:**
- Mathematical complexity that doesn't add insight
- Jargon-heavy presentations
- Complicated frameworks for simple problems
- "Rigor" that obscures rather than clarifies

**Feynman's View:**
> "I couldn't reduce it to the freshman level. That means we really don't understand it."
> — Feynman

**Why:**
Formalism is a tool, not a goal. If the math doesn't help understanding, it's noise. Complexity can hide lack of insight.

---

### 5. What "Everyone Knows"

**Ignore:**
- Accepted wisdom that hasn't been examined
- "Obvious" truths that aren't tested
- Foundational assumptions of the field
- "That's just how it is"

**Feynman's View:**
The most important discoveries often come from questioning what "everyone knows." Hidden assumptions are where breakthroughs hide.

**Why:**
If everyone believes it but no one's tested it, it might be wrong. (See: ulcers caused by "stress" until H. pylori discovery)

---

### 6. Political Considerations

**Ignore:**
- Who benefits from this being true
- What's convenient to believe
- What won't upset people
- Career implications of being right

**Feynman's View:**
> "For a successful technology, reality must take precedence over public relations, for Nature cannot be fooled."
> — Feynman, Rogers Commission Appendix

**Why:**
Nature doesn't care about politics. True understanding requires following evidence wherever it leads, regardless of consequences.

---

## Summary: Attention Allocation

| High Attention | Low Attention |
|----------------|---------------|
| Mechanisms (how it works) | Credentials (who said it) |
| Testable predictions | Unfalsifiable claims |
| Anomalies and exceptions | "What everyone knows" |
| Simplicity under complexity | Impressive formalism |
| Direct evidence | Social conventions |
| Gaps in explanation | Political considerations |

---

## Practical Application

When encountering a new claim or explanation:

```
1. MECHANISM CHECK
   Can they explain HOW it works?
   → If vague: Pay attention (possible gap)

2. TEST CHECK
   Can we verify this?
   → If no: Ignore or hold lightly

3. SIMPLICITY CHECK
   Can they explain it simply?
   → If not: Possible confusion hiding

4. ANOMALY CHECK
   What doesn't fit?
   → If ignored: Pay extra attention

5. SOURCE CHECK
   Can I get closer to raw data?
   → If mediated: Be skeptical
```

---

## The Feynman Filter

**Before investing time in understanding something:**

1. Is it testable? (If no, skip)
2. Is there a mechanism? (If no, be skeptical)
3. Can it be explained simply? (If no, maybe no one understands it)
4. What evidence would change my mind? (If none, it's not knowledge)

> "I'd rather have questions that can't be answered than answers that can't be questioned."
> — Feynman
