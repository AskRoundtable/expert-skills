Mental models from thinkers whose work complements Munger's framework.

## From Nassim Taleb

### Antifragile (反脆弱)

**Source:** Antifragile (反脆弱)

**Definition:** A property of systems that gain from disorder, volatility, and stress—not merely surviving shocks but becoming stronger because of them. This is distinct from "robust" (which resists shocks) and the true opposite of "fragile."

**Application:** When designing systems, careers, or portfolios, ask: "Does this benefit from volatility?" Build structures that improve with stressors rather than merely withstand them. Embrace small failures as opportunities for growth rather than eliminating all variation.

**Example:** The mythical Hydra grows two heads when one is cut off. Bones become stronger under stress. Startups that survive early failures often outperform those with smooth early growth. Wind extinguishes a candle but fuels a fire—be the fire.

---

### Barbell Strategy (槓鈴策略)

**Source:** Antifragile (反脆弱)

**Definition:** Allocate resources to two extremes—extremely conservative AND extremely risky—while avoiding the middle ground. This creates asymmetric exposure: protected downside with unlimited upside potential.

**Application:** In investing: keep 85-90% in ultra-safe assets (cash, T-bills) and 10-15% in highly speculative bets. In career: maintain a stable income source while pursuing high-risk/high-reward side projects. Never be "moderately exposed" to anything.

**Example:** A writer who keeps a day job (extreme safety) while writing experimental novels on weekends (extreme risk) is more antifragile than one who takes a "moderate risk" job at a magazine. The barbell ensures you can't be wiped out while maintaining exposure to Black Swan gains.

---

### Optionality / Convexity (凸性/可選擇性)

**Source:** Antifragile (反脆弱)

**Definition:** Structures with asymmetric payoffs where potential gains significantly exceed potential losses. Options provide the right but not obligation to act, creating limited downside with unlimited upside. Convex positions benefit more from volatility than they lose.

**Application:** Prefer decisions where: (1) the cost of being wrong is small and bounded, (2) the gain from being right is large and unbounded. Avoid situations where you can lose more than you can gain. Collect options—don't sell them.

**Example:** Trying a new restaurant: worst case is a mediocre meal ($50 loss); best case is discovering a lifelong favorite (unlimited upside). Research and development: each failed experiment costs X, but one breakthrough could return 1000X. The tinkerer with many small experiments beats the planner with one big bet.

## From Annie Duke

### 1. Betting Thinking (下注思維)

**Source:** Thinking in Bets (高勝算決策)

Every decision is a bet on the future. Instead of thinking in terms of "right or wrong," think in terms of expected value and probability.

**Core Insight:**
- Decision quality ≠ Outcome quality
- Good decisions can lead to bad outcomes (bad luck)
- Bad decisions can lead to good outcomes (good luck)
- Evaluate decisions by their expected value, not by single outcomes

**Application:**
- Ask: "How much would you bet that this belief is correct?"
- Express confidence in probabilities: "I'm 70% confident" instead of "I'm sure"
- Accept uncertainty as inherent to life decisions

**Diagnostic Question:**
> "If I made this exact decision 100 times, what would be the average outcome?"

---

### 2. Luck vs Skill (區分運氣與技術)

**Source:** Thinking in Bets (高勝算決策)

Life is more like poker than chess. Chess has complete information where outcomes are determined almost entirely by skill. Poker has incomplete information where outcomes = skill x luck.

**Core Insight:**
- Results contain both skill and luck components
- Correct attribution requires separating these factors
- Experience ≠ Expertise; expertise requires correct attribution
- "Bad beat" in poker: good play beaten by luck

**Framework:**
| Situation | Attribution |
|-----------|-------------|
| Good outcome from good decision | Skill + favorable luck |
| Bad outcome from good decision | Skill undermined by bad luck |
| Good outcome from bad decision | Poor skill saved by good luck |
| Bad outcome from bad decision | Poor skill confirmed |

**Application:**
- Before changing strategy after a loss, ask: "Was this luck or a flawed strategy?"
- Track decisions over time to identify true skill vs luck patterns
- Don't overreact to single outcomes

## From Warren Buffett

### Snowball Effect (雪球效應)

**Source:** The Snowball (雪球)

**Definition:** The compound growth principle applied to wealth, knowledge, and relationships. Like a snowball rolling downhill, small consistent actions accumulate into exponential results over time. The key formula: find "wet snow" (good opportunities) and a "long hill" (sufficient time).

**Application:**
- Start early: Buffett began investing at age 11
- Be patient: 60+ years of compounding created the fortune
- Reinvest: Let gains compound rather than withdrawing
- Choose quality: "Wet snow" means opportunities that stick and grow

**Example:** At 26, Buffett had $174,000 and calculated that $12,000/year was enough to live on. He "retired" to let the snowball roll. By 72, his net worth exceeded $60 billion - the power of six decades of compounding.

> "Find wet snow and a long hill."

### Inner Scorecard (內在成績單)

**Source:** The Snowball (雪球)

**Definition:** The practice of measuring success by internal standards rather than external validation. Those with an "inner scorecard" ask: "Am I doing the right thing?" Those with an "outer scorecard" ask: "What will others think?"

**Application:**
- Make decisions based on your own principles, not public opinion
- Avoid "social proof" traps in investing and life decisions
- Build genuine competence rather than the appearance of competence
- Accept short-term criticism when you know you're right

**Example:** During the dot-com bubble, Buffett refused to invest in tech stocks he didn't understand. Wall Street mocked him as outdated. He didn't care - his inner scorecard said "I won't invest in what I don't understand." When the bubble burst, his wisdom was vindicated.

> "Would you rather be the world's greatest lover but have everyone think you're the worst, or be the world's worst lover but have everyone think you're the best?"

---

### Fiduciary Duty (受託人責任)

**Source:** The Snowball (雪球)

**Definition:** The sacred obligation to treat other people's money with more care than your own. A fiduciary must never take risks with entrusted capital that they wouldn't fully disclose and that the beneficiary wouldn't accept.

**Application:**
- Only accept money from people who truly trust you
- Be conservative with others' capital - you can rebuild your own wealth, but not their trust
- Full transparency: disclose risks before, not after, they materialize
- The relationship is asymmetric: their trust is priceless, your management is your duty

**Example:** Buffett's first partnership included his sister Doris ($10,000), Aunt Alice ($35,000), and father-in-law. He said: "If I thought I might lose money, I could never take money from my aunt, my sister, or my father-in-law." This wasn't about returns - it was about honor.

> "If I think I'll lose money, I can't take aunt's money."
