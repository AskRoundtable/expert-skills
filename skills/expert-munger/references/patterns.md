# Charlie Munger: Pattern Library

Situation recognition patterns that Munger identifies instantly. When you spot these patterns, apply the corresponding response.

---

## 1. Incentive Misalignment

**Pattern:** Stated goals conflict with actual incentives. People act against their declared interests.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Compensation structure doesn't match stated goals
- "We want X" but rewards favor Y
- Salespeople paid on volume, not customer satisfaction
- Advisors compensated by product sold, not advice quality

**Munger's Response:**
> "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."
> — Munger, Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting (2003)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- What behavior does the incentive actually reward?
- Who benefits from the current structure?
- What would happen if we aligned incentives with goals?

**Example:**
Mortgage brokers in 2008 were paid to close loans, not ensure repayment. Incentive → outcome: they closed bad loans.

**Source:** Munger, C. (2005). *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, p. 75-77.

---

## 2. Lollapalooza Effect

**Pattern:** Multiple psychological biases or forces combine and reinforce each other, creating an extreme outcome.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Several biases operating simultaneously
- Social proof + authority + commitment
- Outcome seems disproportionate to any single cause
- "Perfect storm" situations

**Munger's Response:**
> "When three or four forces are all operating in the same direction, you don't get a simple sum—you get a nuclear explosion."
> — Munger, Harvard Law School (1995)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- How many psychological forces are at play here?
- Are they reinforcing or opposing each other?
- What's the second-order effect of their combination?

**Example:**
Tupperware parties: reciprocity (host gave you food) + social proof (friends buying) + liking (buying from a friend) + commitment (you said you'd come) = sales far beyond rational product evaluation.

**Source:** Munger, C. (1995). "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment." Harvard Law School.

## 3. Circle of Competence Violation

**Pattern:** Someone (including yourself) is making confident decisions outside their area of expertise.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Can't explain the thing simply
- Relying on others' opinions without verification
- Unfamiliar domain + high confidence
- "Trust me, I've done my research" (but can't explain how it fails)

**Munger's Response:**
> "Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant."
> — Munger, USC Law Commencement (2007)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Can I explain how this works to a child?
- Do I understand how it fails, not just how it succeeds?
- Am I relying on borrowed conviction?
- What don't I know that I don't know?

**Example:**
Tech executives investing in biotech based on "hot tips" without understanding FDA approval processes, clinical trial statistics, or competitive dynamics.

**Source:** Munger, C. (2005). *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, p. 58-60.

---

## 4. Envy/Jealousy

**Pattern:** Decisions driven by comparison to others rather than absolute merit.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "But he got X, why can't I?"
- Status focus over substance
- Keeping up with competitors/neighbors/peers
- Resentment of others' success driving action

**Munger's Response:**
> "The world is not driven by greed. It's driven by envy."
> — Munger, Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting (2016)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Would I want this if no one knew I had it?
- Am I comparing to others or to my own goals?
- Is this decision about utility or status?

**Example:**
Hedge fund managers taking excessive risk because rivals had a better year—not because the opportunity warranted it.

**Source:** Munger, C. (2005). *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, p. 94-95.

---

## 5. Social Proof Cascade

**Pattern:** People copying others' actions without independent analysis. The more people do X, the more others assume X is correct.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "Everyone is doing it"
- Rapid adoption without clear rationale
- Behavior spreading through imitation, not analysis
- Discomfort being the only one not participating

**Munger's Response:**
> "Whenever you find yourself on the side of the majority, it is time to pause and reflect."
> — Munger (paraphrasing Mark Twain)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- Would I do this if I were the only one?
- What's the independent case for this action?
- Am I following the crowd or my own analysis?

**Example:**
Dot-com bubble: investors bought tech stocks because others were buying, not because of fundamentals. Social proof replaced analysis.

**Source:** Munger, C. (1995). "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment." Harvard Law School.

---

## Pattern Recognition Process

When analyzing a situation:

1. **Scan for patterns:** Does this match any known prototype?
2. **Check multiple patterns:** Often more than one applies
3. **Note what's missing:** What pattern should be here but isn't?
4. **Apply the response:** Use the diagnostic questions
5. **Watch for Lollapalooza:** Are patterns combining?
