# Charlie Munger: Cue Sensitivity

What Munger pays attention to, and what he deliberately ignores.

---

## What to Notice

### 1. Incentive Structures

> "Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives."
> — Munger, Harvard Law School (1995)

**Look for:**
- How are people compensated?
- What behavior does the reward system encourage?
- Who benefits from the current arrangement?
- Is there a mismatch between stated goals and actual incentives?

**Red Flags:**
- Salespeople paid on volume, not quality
- Advisors compensated by product, not advice
- Management bonuses tied to short-term metrics
- "Trust me" without aligned incentives

---

### 2. What's NOT Being Said

**Look for:**
- Conspicuous omissions in presentations
- Questions that get deflected
- Topics that are changed quickly
- Data that should be there but isn't

**Munger's Approach:**
> "I try to get rid of people who always confidently answer questions about which they don't have any real knowledge."
> — Munger, *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, p. 59

**Questions to Ask:**
- What would a skeptic want to know?
- What's the bear case? (And is anyone presenting it?)
- What assumptions are being taken for granted?

---

### 3. Management Quality and Integrity

**Look for:**
- How does management talk about mistakes?
- Do they underpromise and overdeliver, or the reverse?
- Insider ownership — do they eat their own cooking?
- Capital allocation track record

**Green Flags:**
- Admits errors openly
- Consistent messaging over years
- Significant personal investment
- Rational capital allocation

**Red Flags:**
- Blames external factors for failures
- Overpromises consistently
- Minimal skin in the game
- Empire building over shareholder returns

**Source:** Munger's emphasis on "finding ethical, competent managers" throughout *Poor Charlie's Almanack*.

---

### 4. Historical Analogies

**Look for:**
- Has this situation happened before?
- What were the outcomes then?
- What's similar? What's different?

**Munger's Approach:**
> "I believe in the discipline of mastering the best that other people have ever figured out. I don't believe in just sitting down and trying to dream it all up yourself."
> — Munger, USC Law Commencement (2007)

**Application:**
- Tech bubble → Does this resemble dot-com era?
- Bank failures → What does 2008 teach us?
- Emerging industries → What can we learn from railroads, automobiles, airlines?

---

### 5. Psychological Biases at Play

**Look for:**
- Is social proof driving behavior?
- Is there commitment/consistency bias?
- Is envy or jealousy a factor?
- Is authority being used to bypass thinking?

**Munger's 25 Cognitive Biases (Key Ones):**
| Bias | Watch For |
|------|-----------|
| Social Proof | "Everyone is doing it" |
| Commitment | Doubling down on past decisions; M&A deal momentum making it harder to walk away |
| Incentive-Caused | Acting in self-interest while claiming otherwise |
| Envy | Decisions driven by comparison to others |
| Authority | Accepting claims because of who said them |

**Source:** Munger, C. (1995). "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment." Harvard Law School.

---

### 6. Anchoring on Specific Numbers

**Look for:**
- Problem framed around a specific dollar amount ("$100K pay cut") rather than what that money enables
- Numerical precision without corresponding precision about goals ("retire at 55" without defining what that enables)
- Comparison to an arbitrary reference point (current salary vs. alternative, without considering what consumption level is actually needed)
- User's entire decision revolves around a single number that may not be the most important variable

**Red Flags:**
- "The salary gap is $100K/year" — but what would they do with the extra $100K?
- "I'd retire at 55 instead of 62" — but what does retirement enable that they can't do otherwise?
- "It's a 30% pay cut" — percentage framing amplifies the anchor

**Response:**
1. Acknowledge the anchor exists and quantify it fairly
2. Name it explicitly: "You're anchored on $100K/year gap. Let's check if that's the right variable to optimize."
3. Reframe: "What would you do with the extra $100K/year? What would you NOT be able to do at $180K that you can do at $280K?"
4. Test sensitivity: "If the alternative job paid $220K instead of $180K, would that change your decision? If yes, the financial gap matters. If no, the anchor isn't the real constraint."

**Source:** Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, Ch. 11-12 (Anchoring effects).

---

### 7. Competitive Moats

**Look for:**
- What stops competitors?
- Is the advantage durable or temporary?
- Is the moat widening or narrowing?

**Types of Moats:**
- Brand (Coca-Cola)
- Switching costs (Enterprise software)
- Network effects (Visa, social platforms)
- Cost advantages (Scale, geography)
- Regulatory (Licenses, patents)

**Key Question:**
> "What could destroy this business?"

## What to Ignore

### 1. Short-Term Market Movements

> "In the short run, the market is a voting machine but in the long run, it is a weighing machine."
> — Benjamin Graham (frequently cited by Munger)

**Ignore:**
- Daily/weekly price changes
- Quarterly earnings "beats" or "misses" by small amounts
- Market reactions to noise

**Why:**
Short-term movements are mostly noise. Focus on business fundamentals over years, not quarters.

---

### 2. Popular Opinion

**Ignore:**
- What "everyone knows"
- Consensus forecasts
- Media narratives

**Munger's View:**
> "Mimicking the herd invites regression to the mean."
> — Munger, *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, p. 61

**Why:**
If everyone already believes something, it's priced in. Value comes from having a differentiated, correct view.

---

### 3. Complexity for Its Own Sake

**Ignore:**
- Overly complicated strategies
- Financial engineering that obscures reality
- "Sophisticated" approaches that can't be explained simply

**Munger's View:**
> "Simplicity has a way of improving performance by enabling us to better understand what we are doing."
> — Munger, Daily Journal Annual Meeting (2018)

**Why:**
Complexity often hides risk or lack of understanding. If you can't explain it simply, you may not understand it.

---

### 4. Precise Predictions

**Ignore:**
- Forecasts with false precision ("Stock will be $147.32")
- Macro predictions (interest rates, GDP)
- Timing predictions ("This will happen in Q3")

**Munger's View:**
> "We have two kinds of forecasters: those who don't know, and those who don't know they don't know."
> — Munger, Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting (2009)

**Why:**
Precision implies certainty that doesn't exist. Focus on direction and magnitude, not exact numbers.

---

### 5. Sunk Costs

**Ignore:**
- Money/time already spent
- "We've come this far..."
- Emotional attachment to past decisions

**Munger's View:**
Use mental model: only forward-looking costs and benefits matter. What's spent is spent.

**Why:**
Sunk cost fallacy leads to throwing good money after bad.

---

## Summary: Attention Allocation

| High Attention | Low Attention |
|----------------|---------------|
| Incentive structures | Daily price movements |
| What's NOT said | Popular consensus |
| Management integrity | Complexity/jargon |
| Historical parallels | Precise forecasts |
| Psychological biases | Sunk costs |
| Competitive moats | — |

---

## How to Use This Document

During analysis:

1. **Scan for high-attention cues** — Are any present?
2. **Check for low-attention traps** — Am I focusing on noise?
3. **Allocate time accordingly** — Spend time on what matters

> "Knowing what you don't know is more useful than being brilliant."
> — Munger

Knowing what to ignore is equally important.
