# Decision Quality Patterns

Patterns for evaluating and improving decision quality, primarily from Annie Duke's work.

---

## 1. Resulting (結果論)

**Source:** Thinking in Bets (高勝算決策)

Judging decision quality purely by outcomes, ignoring the decision-making process and the role of luck.

**Description:**
Resulting is the tendency to evaluate a decision based on its outcome rather than the quality of the decision-making process at the time it was made. This is dangerous because:
- Good decisions can produce bad outcomes due to bad luck
- Bad decisions can produce good outcomes due to good luck
- Using outcomes to judge decisions corrupts our learning feedback loop

**Example:**
Pete Carroll's pass play in Super Bowl XLIX that was intercepted. The world called it a "stupid decision." But pre-decision analysis showed the pass had positive expected value. The bad outcome doesn't make it a bad decision.

**Trigger Cues:**
- "That was a bad decision" (said after seeing outcome)
- "I knew that would happen" (hindsight bias)
- Changing strategy immediately after one bad outcome
- Praising decisions only because they worked out

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Am I evaluating this decision based on information available at decision time, or information revealed after?
2. Would I call this a good decision if the outcome had been different?
3. If this decision were made 100 times, what would be the distribution of outcomes?

## 2. Outcome Bias (結果偏誤)

**Source:** Thinking in Bets (高勝算決策)

The systematic error of believing that a good outcome means the decision was good, and a bad outcome means the decision was bad.

**Description:**
Outcome bias is the cognitive tendency to conflate decision quality with result quality. It prevents proper learning because:
- We reinforce bad processes that got lucky
- We abandon good processes that got unlucky
- We fail to identify what actually drives good decisions

**Relationship to Resulting:**
Resulting is the behavior; Outcome Bias is the underlying cognitive bias that causes it.

**Example:**
A poker player believes "suited connectors always win" based on memorable wins, but has never tracked actual win/loss statistics. The vivid good outcomes bias the assessment.

**Trigger Cues:**
- Drawing conclusions from single events
- Vivid memories of outcomes influencing beliefs
- "It worked last time" without statistical backing
- Ignoring base rates and sample sizes

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Have I tracked data on this, or am I relying on memorable outcomes?
2. What's my sample size? Is it large enough to draw conclusions?
3. Am I separating the process from the result in my evaluation?
4. Would a "truth-seeking group" agree with my attribution?
